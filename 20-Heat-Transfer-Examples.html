Heat Transfer Examples

A 10 m pipe with a outer-radius of $r_{pipe}=2.5$ cm is to be insulated using a layer of insulation with a thermal conductivity of $k=0.18$ W/m K. You may assume that the external convective heat transfer coefficient of the insulation is constant at $h=5$ W/m ${}^2$ K and that these two mechanisms are the only significant heat transfer resistances.
  1. Write down the heat transfer equation for this system showing how the overall heat transfer rate$Q$ depends on $k$, $r_{pipe}$, the outer radius of the pipe insulation $r_{ins.}$, the pipe length $L$, and the temperature difference $\Delta T$ between the pipe wall and the ambient air.
    Note: The resistance to heat transfer in a cylindrical shell is: \begin{align*} R = \frac{\ln\left(r_{outer}/r_{inner}\right)}{2 \pi k L} \end{align*} The external convection resistance to heat transfer is \begin{align*} R_{conv.}= \frac{1}{2 \pi r_{ins.} L h} \end{align*} Summing the resistances, we have \begin{align*} Q = \Delta T/\left(\frac{\ln\left(r_{ins.}/r_{pipe}\right)}{2 \pi k L} + \frac{1}{2 \pi r_{ins.} L h}\right) \end{align*}
A 10 m pipe with a outer-radius of $r_{pipe}=2.5$ cm is to be insulated using a layer of insulation with a thermal conductivity of $k=0.18$ W/m K. You may assume that the external convective heat transfer coefficient of the insulation is constant at $h=5$ W/m ${}^2$ K and that these two mechanisms are the only significant heat transfer resistances.
  1. ii) Calculate the heat transfer rate for three thicknesses of insulation $r_{ins.}=3.0$ cm, 3.6 cm, and 4.2 cm. The surface temperature of the pipe is $400^\circ$ C and ambient conditions are at $10^\circ$ C. Substituting in the known values, we have \begin{align*} Q &= 2 \pi L \Delta T / \left(\frac{\ln\left(r_{ins.}/r_{pipe}\right)}{k} + \frac{1}{r_{ins.} h}\right)\\ &= 2 \pi 10\times(400-10) / \left(\frac{\ln\left(r_{ins.}/0.025\right)}{0.18} + \frac{1}{r_{ins.}\times5}\right)\\ &= 24500 / (\ln\left(r_{ins.}/0.025\right)/0.18 + 0.2/r_{ins.})\\ &= \begin{cases} 3190 \text{W} & \text{for $r_{ins.}=3.0$}\\ 3231 \text{W} & \text{for $r_{ins.}=3.6$}\\ 3205 \text{W} & \text{for $r_{ins.}=4.2$}\\ \end{cases} \end{align*}
  1. iii) Explain why you observe a maximum in the heat transfer rate. There is a maximum in the heat transfer rate as, at first, the resistance to convection decreases faster than the resistance to conduction increases. Eventually the conduction resistance dominates. The critical radius can be derived as follows: \begin{align*} \frac{\partial Q}{\partial r} &= 2 \pi L \Delta T \frac{\partial}{\partial r} 1 / \left(\frac{\ln\left(r_{ins.}/r_{pipe}\right)}{k} + \frac{1}{r_{ins.} h}\right) \end{align*} Using the chain rule the derivative is \begin{align*} \frac{\partial Q}{\partial r} &= -2 \pi L \Delta T \left(\frac{\ln\left(r_{ins.}/r_{pipe}\right)}{k} + \frac{1}{r_{ins.} h}\right)^{-2} \left(\frac{1}{r_{ins.} k} - \frac{1}{r_{ins.}^2 h}\right) \end{align*} This derivative is zero when \begin{align*} \left(\frac{1}{r_{ins.} k} - \frac{1}{r_{ins.}^2 h}\right)&=0 \end{align*} Rearranging for the critical insulation radius gives \begin{align*} r_c = \frac{k}{h} \end{align*} In this case the critical radius is $r_c=0.18/5=0.036$ m or 3.6 cm.
A black-body car is left in direct sunlight at midday, which can be approximated as a constant heat flux $q_{sun}=1000$ W/m ${}^2$. The car's surface temperature reaches steady state with its surroundings and is approximately constant. The car has a surface area of 26 m ${}^2$ but only $8$ m ${}^2$ are exposed to sunlight.
  1. Assuming that the ambient temperature is 15${}^\circ$ C and that radiation is the only heat transfer mechanism, calculate the surface temperature of the car. Is the estimate realistic?
    At steady state, the flux of energy into the car from the sunlight is equal to the energy lost through radiation: \begin{align*} A_{sun} q_{sun}=-A_{car} q_{rad}=\sigma A_{car} \varepsilon\left(T^4_{car}-T^4_\infty\right) \end{align*} We have $\varepsilon=1$ as the car is black and $\sigma=5.6703\times10^{-8}$ W/m ${}^2$ K ${}^4$ from the data sheet. Substituting in the known values, we have \begin{align*} 8\times1000&=26\times5.6703\times10^{-8}\left(T^4_{car}-288.15^4\right)\\ T^4_{car}&=\frac{8000}{26\times5.6703\times10^{-8}}+288.15^4\\ T_{car}&=333 K = 60 {{}^\circ}C \end{align*} This temperature is fairly realistic for cars in the UK on hot summer days.
  1. ii) Using the previous estimate for the surface temperature ( $T_{car}=333$ K), estimate the heat flux due to natural convection from the sides of the car and comment on its magnitude. You may approximate the sides of the car as a vertical wall 12 m wide and 1.5 m high. You may assume the following properties of air at these conditions.
    $\rho$ (kg/m ${}^3$ ) $k$ (W/m K) $\mu$ (kg/m s) $C_p$ (J/mol K) Mol. Weight (g)
    1.225 0.026 $1.827\times10^{-5}$ 29.19 29
    First we must calculate the Grashof number, but we need the thermal expansion coefficient, $\beta$. We can quickly derive it from the ideal gas equation and the identity in the datasheet \begin{align*} \beta&=\frac{1}{V}\frac{\partial V}{\partial T} =\frac{1}{V}\frac{\partial}{\partial T}\frac{n R T}{P} =\frac{n R}{P V} =\frac{1}{T} \end{align*} Or simply remember that $\beta=1/T$ for an ideal gas. This must be evaluated at the film temperature $T_f=(T_{wall}+T_\infty)/2=(60+15)/2=37.5 {}^\circ$ C $=311$ K. The $L$ term in the Grashof number is the plate height, as the height is the characteristic length for convection.

    \begin{align*} \text{Gr}&=\frac{g \rho^2 \beta\left(T_w-T_\infty\right) L^3}{\mu^2}\\ &=\frac{9.81\times1.225^2\times311^{-1}(60-15)1.5^3}{\left(1.827\times 10^{-5}\right)^2}\\ &\approx2.15\times10^{10} \end{align*} Converting $C_p$ to kJ/kg, we have $C_p=29.19/29=1.007$ kJ/kg K. Calculating the Prandtl number \begin{align*} \text{Pr}=\frac{\mu C_p}{k}=\frac{1.827\times10^{-5}\times1.007\times10^3}{0.026}\approx0.71 \end{align*} The Rayleigh number is then \begin{align*} \text{Ra}=\text{Pr} \text{Gr}=0.71\times2.15\times10^{10}\approx1.53\times10^{10} \end{align*} Looking in the data sheet, this corresponds to the following expression for the Nusselt number \begin{align*} \text{Nu}=0.13\left(\text{Ra}\right)^{1/3}=0.13\times\left(1.53\times10^{10}\right)^{1/3}\approx323 \end{align*} This gives a heat transfer coefficient of \begin{align*} h = \frac{\text{Nu} k}{L}=\frac{323\times0.026}{1.5}\approx5.60 \text{W/m${}^2$K} \end{align*} The convective heat transfer is then \begin{align*} Q_{conv.}=h A \left(T_{wall}-T_\infty\right)=5.60\times1.5\times12\left(60-15\right)=4.536 \text{kW} \end{align*} The natural convective heat transfer is relatively large compared to the total radiant heat transfer ( $8000$ W), therefore iterations are needed to determine the true surface temperature.
  2. Discuss how you might improve the accuracy of the calculations, and what the effect of setting the car in motion will be. The accuracy may be improved by finding a better approximation for the car surface for the convection calculations, specifying realistic emissivities for the car surface, and solving for the radiation and convection fluxes simultaneously. If the car is set in motion, the natural convection will become a forced convection, greatly increasing the heat transfer rate of this mode. It is likely that this will cause the car surface to significantly cool.
