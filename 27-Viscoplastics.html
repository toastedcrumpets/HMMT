Viscoplastics

  • Before we consider viscoplastic materials, we must make a general observation.
  • If we have a steady-state flow, moving through a closed channel, we must have a driving pressure gradient $\Delta P$.
  • This pressure gradient is the force which is keeping the fluid in motion.
  • If the flow is not accelerating, this driving force must be opposed by an equal and opposite drag force.
  • This opposing force is imparted on the flow through the boundaries.
  • The fluid next to the wall cannot slip against the fixed boundaries and this causes viscous stresses to appear.
  • This is a general statement that the force from the pressure gradient is equal to the viscous stresses on the boundaries !
  • For a steady state flow through a closed channel, the pressure force is given by \begin{align*} F_{pressure} &= p_{in} A_{flow,in} - p_{out} A_{flow,out}\\ &= -\Delta p  A_{flow} \end{align*} where $A_{flow}$ is the cross sectional area of the flow and we have assumed this is the same at both ends of the channel.
  • The viscous forces at the boundaries are equal to the area of the boundaries multiplied by the stress at the boundary/wall. \begin{align*} F_{viscous} &= \tau_{boundary} A_{boundary}\\ &=\tau_{boundary} L P_w \end{align*} where $A_{boundary}$ is the area of the boundary, $L$ is the length of the channel and $P_w$ is the wetted perimeter.
  • Balancing the forces gives us \begin{align*} F_{viscous} + F_{pressure} &= \tau_{boundary} L P_w - \Delta P A_{flow} = 0\\ \tau_{boundary} = \frac{\Delta p A_{flow}}{L P_w} \end{align*}
\begin{align*} \tau_{boundary} = \frac{\Delta p A_{flow}}{L P_w} \end{align*}
  • This is a general statement that is true regardless of the flow-configuration, viscous behaviour or velocity profile of the fluid.
  • We can use this as a starting point to derive empirical relationships for fluids where we can't fit a function to their viscous behaviour.
  • It is also very useful when calculating at what pressure a viscoplastic fluid will start to flow.
  • There are many substances which are viscoplastic.
  • These are substances which are fluid above a certain yield stress $\tau_0$ but are solid below the critical stress.
  • A simple example is mayonnaise, but these fluids occur everywhere.
  • An example relevant for Aberdeen is drilling mud used offshore for clearing cuttings from a new well/maintaining pressure or gelled oil flows (see the paper from last week).
  • They have a common characteristic of appearing as a “frozen flow.”
  • Many viscoplastic flows are also thixotropic, or time dependant.
  • The viscosity of the flow is a function of the current and previous shear-rates.
  • An example of this is jelly, it sets (increases in viscosity) if you don't shear it and breaks up if you do shear it.
  • This time dependant behaviour presents a real challenge in modelling and is difficult to capture.
  • But, luckily, it usually doesn't play a part in industrially relevant problems as flow calculations are only a problem in long pipes or flows.
  • Here we can usually assume the conditions along the pipe are constant and any short-time dependence is gone.
  • There are many models for viscoplastic behaviour. However, the simplest viscous model to use is the Bingham-Plastic.
  • This model states that the fluid doesn't flow until a critical yield stress $\tau_0$ is reached.
  • Once this yield stress is reached, the fluid then flows as a Newtonian fluid.
  • Mathematically this is expressed as \begin{align*} \frac{\partial v_x}{\partial y} = \begin{cases} -\mu^{-1}\left(\tau_{xy}-\tau_0)\right) & \text{if $\tau_{xy} > \tau_0 $} \\ 0 & \text{if $\tau_{xy}\leq\tau_0 $} \end{cases} \end{align*}
\begin{align*} \frac{\partial v_x}{\partial y} = \begin{cases} -\mu^{-1}\left(\tau_{xy}-\tau_0)\right) & \text{if $\tau_{xy} > \tau_0 $} \\ 0 & \text{if $\tau_{xy}\leq\tau_0 $} \end{cases} \end{align*}
  • This model is not very realistic as the transition from solid to fluid is too sharp.
  • The instant change to Newtonian flow is not seen in real systems.
  • A shear-thinning Power-Law behaviour, once the yield stress is reached, is much more realistic (dotted).
  • However, this model will present us with all of the mathematical challenges we need to worry about. All other models will be worked out in a similar fashion.
  • Let's take a simple example such as the flow of a Bingham plastic in a pipe…
  • Immediately, we can use the expression we derived earlier for the viscous stress at the wall in terms of the pressure drop. \begin{align*} \tau_{boundary} = \frac{\Delta p A_{flow}}{L P_w} \end{align*}
  • For a pipe, the wetted perimeter is given by the pipe circumference $P_w=2\pi R$ and the flow area is $A_{flow}=\pi R^2$.
  • The stress on the boundary is then given by \begin{align*} \tau_{boundary} = \frac{\Delta p R}{2 L} \end{align*}
  • The stresses at the boundaries are the maximum stresses in the system! \begin{align*} \tau_{boundary} = \frac{\Delta p R}{2 L} \end{align*}
  • If these stresses are less than the yield stress $\tau_{boundary} \leq \tau_0$, the fluid will not flow!
  • We can also rearrange this to calculate a critical “yield” pressure drop $\Delta p_0$. \begin{align*} \Delta p_0 = \frac{2 \tau_0 L}{R} \end{align*}
  • We note that, as always, the stress profile is always the same for a given flow geometry. Even if the flowing fluid might be part solid. \begin{align*} \tau_{rz} = \left(\frac{\Delta p}{L}-\rho g_z\right)\frac{r}{2} \end{align*}
  • We also note that at $r=R$, and in the absence of gravity, this is the same expression that we derived for the boundary stress \begin{align*} \tau_{boundary} = \frac{\Delta p R}{2 L} \end{align*}
  • Let's assume that the wall stresses are above the critical stress, $\tau_{boundary}>\tau_0$ and therefore $\Delta p>\Delta p_0$.
  • The flow will be part-fluid and part-solid, depending on the shear stress.
  • The first thing we need to do is identify where the shear stress $\tau_{rz}$ in the system drops below the yield stress using the stress profile \begin{align*} \tau_{rz} = \frac{\Delta p}{L}\frac{r}{2} \end{align*}
  • When $\tau_{rz}=\tau_0$, the fluid becomes a solid.
  • This is a straightforward rearrangement to determine the critical radius $r_0$. \begin{align*} r_0 = \frac{2 \tau_0 L}{\Delta p} \end{align*}
  • We can now divide our problem into two parts.
    • A fluid region with a velocity profile.
    • A solid region with a constant velocity $v_{z,0}$.
  • Starting with the fluid region.
  • In a Bingham plastic, the stress-strain relation is \begin{align*} \frac{\partial v_z}{\partial r} = \begin{cases} -\mu^{-1}\left(\tau_{rz}-\tau_0)\right) &(\tau_{xy} > \tau_0) \\ 0 & (\tau_{xy} \leq \tau_0) \end{cases} \end{align*}
  • In the fluid region of the flow, $\tau_{rz}>\tau_0$ and we only need the first definition.
  • And our stress profile is given by \begin{align*} \tau_{rz} = \frac{\Delta p}{L}\frac{r}{2} \end{align*}
  • We can use these equations to get this expression for the flow velocity in the liquid region \begin{align*} \frac{\partial v_z}{\partial r} &= \mu^{-1}\left(\tau_0 - \frac{\Delta p r}{2 L}\right) \end{align*}
  • Integrating, we have \begin{align*} v_z &= \mu^{-1}\left(\tau_0 r - \frac{\Delta p r^2}{4 L}\right) + C \end{align*}
  • We know that at $r=R$, the no slip condition states $v_z=0$ and solving for the constant gives \begin{align*} v_z &= \mu^{-1}\left(\frac{\Delta p}{4 L}\left(R^2 - r^2\right) - \tau_0 (R-r)\right) \end{align*}
  • Now we have the flow profile, we want to work out the volumetric flow rate.
  • First, we need the velocity of the solid plug.
  • Using the no-slip condition, we have $v_z=v_{z,0}$ at $r=r_0$ where \begin{align*} r_0 = \frac{2 \tau_0 L}{\Delta p} \end{align*}
  • Substituting this into the velocity profile we have (after a lot of algebra) \begin{align*} v_{z,0} &= \frac{\Delta p R^2}{8 \mu L}\left(2 - 4  X + 2 X^2\right) \end{align*} where $X=\frac{2 \tau_0 L}{\Delta p  R}$.
  • The volume of fluid flowing in the solid plug is given by the plug velocity times by the plug's cross sectional area \begin{align*} \dot{V}_{plug} &= v_{z,0} \pi r_0^2\\ &= \frac{\Delta p \pi R^2}{8 \mu L}\left(2 X^2 - 4  X^3 + 2 X^4\right) \end{align*}
  • We need to find the volumetric flow rate in the fluid.
  • We get this by integrating the flow velocity over the annular shell region \begin{align*} \dot{V}_{liquid} &= \int_{r_0}^R \int_0^{2 \pi}v_z r {\rm d}\theta {\rm d}r \end{align*}
  • After more working out we get the volumetric flow-rate to be: \begin{align*} \dot{V}_{liquid} &= \frac{\Delta p \pi R^4}{8 \mu L}\left(1-\frac{4}{3}X - 2 X^2+4 X^3 - \frac{5}{3}X^4\right) \end{align*}
  • The total flow rate is given by the sum of the fluid and solid flows \begin{align*} \dot{V} &= \dot{V}_{liquid} + \dot{V}_{solid}\\ &= \frac{\Delta p R^2}{8 \mu L}\left(1- \frac{4}{3} X + \frac{1}{3}X^4\right) \end{align*} where $X=\frac{2 \tau_0 L}{\Delta p  R}$
  • This is another famous equation, known as Buckingham's equation.
  • Here we have hit a problem. This is a fourth order equation in the pressure drop.
  • If we wanted to rearrange this equation to generate a friction factor (needed in the next set of slides to generalise the Reynold's number), we would need to find the solution to a quartic.
  • Luckily for you, there is only a stable quadratic formula and cubic formula, so quartics are out of our grasp for now.
  • However, there are many approximate solutions available in the literature.
  • We now know enough about non-newtonian flows to sketch four types of profile (see above).
  • We have found a way to calculate the boundary stresses as a function of the pressure drop. \begin{align*} \tau_{boundary} = \frac{\Delta p A_{flow}}{L P_w} \end{align*}
  • We have found that Bingham plastics have a yield stress, and using the boundary stress values we can also calculate a minimum pressure for the fluid to flow by setting $\tau_{boundary}=\tau_0$.
  • We have calculated the volumetric flow profile of a Bingham plastic fluid in a pipe and learnt the following important concepts
    • Using the stress profile we can identify the size of the solid core which is surrounded by a liquid annulus.
    • We can use the no-slip boundary condition to determine the velocities across a phase boundary!
    • We can calculate the volumetric flow rate of a multi-phase system by summing up the flowrates of the two separate flows.
  • We can sketch the flow profiles for a range of non-Newtonian flow types.
