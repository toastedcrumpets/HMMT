Differential Balance Equations

  • Now that our math is sufficiently revised we can begin to derive the tools of Transport Phenomena (AKA Heat, Mass, and Momentum Transfer).
  • The first mathematical tool in the toolbox of Transport Phenomena is actually the most important equation of Chemical Engineering…
The fundamental equation of chemical engineering is arguably the balance equation : \begin{align*} \textrm{INPUT}-\textrm{OUTPUT}+\textrm{GENERATION}=\textrm{ACCUMULATION} \end{align*}
  • This equation can be used to study the dynamics of any “slow”/low-fluctuation quantity, such as
    • Mass/Concentration.
    • Momentum.
    • Energy/Heat.
    • Money/Investment.
\begin{align*} \textrm{INPUT}-\textrm{OUTPUT}+\textrm{GENERATION}=\textrm{ACCUMULATION} \end{align*}
  • In your course on Process Engineering, you will have applied the balance equation to understand the flow of material and energy through process equipment and entire plants.
  • As energy and mass are conserved, a balance can be used to find out on a flow sheet where the mass and energy of a process plant actually are.
  • It is also particularly useful when looking at a single unit operation as a black-box.
  • For example, let us take a look at a heater…
\begin{align*} \textrm{INPUT} - \textrm{OUTPUT} + \cancelto{0}{\textrm{GENERATION}}&= \cancelto{0}{\textrm{ACCUMULATION}} \\ \overbrace{Q + \dot{m} C_p T_{in}}^{\text{INPUT}} - \overbrace{\dot{m} C_p T_{out}}^{\text{OUTPUT}}&= 0 \end{align*}
  • This allows a rough estimate of the heat exchanger duty, $Q$, to be calculated from the temperature change of the fluid (assuming constant heat capacity1).
  • But what if we want to perform a detailed design and design inside the unit operation? What about differences in flow configuration?
1 The proper energy balance is $Q = \dot{m}\int_{T_{in}}^{T_{out}} Cp(T) {\rm d}T$
  • Heat exchangers which exchange heat between two fluids can be run in co-current or counter-current configurations.
  • In this case we can use the Log Mean Temperature Difference (LMTD) combined with the balance equations for each stream individually. \begin{align*} Q &= U\times A\times LMTD = \dot{m}_1 C_{p,1} \Delta T_1= -\dot{m}_2 C_{p,2} \Delta T_2\\ LMTD &= \left(\Delta T_A - \Delta T_B\right)/\ln\left(\Delta T_A/\Delta T_B\right) \end{align*}
  • These design equations allow us to estimate the heat transfer area of the exchanger.
  • But how did we derive this expression and what are its limitations? Can we find more general expressions for more complex systems?
  • We wish to describe the transport of some property, be it mass, momentum, and/or energy.
  • We want to model these transport processes at the smallest resolution possible, so that we can calculate temperatures, pressures, etc. within a unit process.
  • The solution is to create a differential balance!
  • This is a balance of some property (mass, energy, momentum) over a vanishingly small volume of the system, known as a control volume.
  • Just as the balance equation works for larger volumes, it also works for vanishing volumes.
  • The resulting balance equations will describe the transport at every point in the system, not just the overall transport.
  • We create differential balances just as we create normal balances.
  • For example, you have a bank account which earns interest at a continuous1 and compounding rate $I$, proportional to your current balance $B(t)$.
  • Performing a balance over a small interval of time, $\Delta t$ , we have. \begin{align*} \textrm{ACCUMULATION} &= \cancelto{0}{\textrm{INPUT}} - \cancelto{0}{\textrm{OUTPUT}} + \textrm{GENERATION} \\ \overbrace{B(t+\Delta t) - B(t)}^{\text{ACCUMULATION}} &= \overbrace{I B(t) \Delta t}^{\text{GENERATION}} \\ \frac{B(t+\Delta t) - B(t)}{\Delta t} &= I B \end{align*}
    A definition of a first derivative is:
    \begin{align*} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x} &= \lim_{\Delta x\to 0}\frac{f(x+\Delta x)-f(x)}{\Delta x} \end{align*} We use a better definition later...
1 Real compounding interest is typically calculated on a daily basis, so it is not continuous but it will appear continuous after a large number of days.
  • Taking the limit of $\Delta t\to0$ , we have \begin{align*} \frac{{\rm d}B(t)}{{\rm d}t} &= I B \end{align*}
  • Integrating, we have \begin{align*} \int \frac{1}{B} {\rm d}B(t) &= I  \int  {\rm d}t\\ B &= C_2 \exp(I t) \quad \text{(where $C_2=\exp(C_1) $)} \end{align*}
  • Assuming that your account starts at £$100$ , solving for $B(t=0)=$ £100 gives $C_2=$ £100.
  • If your account has an annual interest rate of 10% APR, Solving for $B(t=1$ yr $)=$ £ $110$ we have $I = \ln(1.1)$: \begin{align*} B &= 100 \exp(\ln(1.1) t) \end{align*}
  • “Compound interest is the eighth wonder of the world. Those who understand it, earns it ... those who don't ... pay it.” - Anon.
  • To create engineering balances, we'll need to use the input and output (fluxes) as well as the generation/accumulation terms.
  • To keep this general, we will look at an example of a differential balance for an arbitrary property, $B$, in one dimension.
  • $B$ may be the energy, mass, or momentum in the system.
  • The one-dimensional system may be along the axis of a pipe.
  • We can define $J_B(x)$ to be the flux of $B$ (the flow rate of units of $B$ per unit area) at some point, $x$, in the system.
  • We also can define that at any point, $x$, in the system, $B$ is being generated at a rate of $\sigma_B(x)$ units of $B$ per unit volume.
  • Now we take a small volume of the system, around the point $x$ and perform a balance…
\begin{align*} \textrm{INPUT}-\textrm{OUTPUT}+\textrm{GENERATION}&=\textrm{ACCUMULATION}\\ \Delta t A J_B(x)-\Delta t A J_B(x+\Delta x)+\Delta t \Delta V \sigma_B(x) &= \Delta V c_B(t+\Delta t) - \Delta V c_B(t) \end{align*}
$A$ : Cross sectional area of the volume.
$x$ : Position of the control volume.
$\sigma_B$ : Production of $B$ at $x$ .
$\Delta t$ : Time.
$\Delta x$ : Width of the volume.
$c_B$ : Concentration of $B$ at $x$ .
$J_B$ : Flux of $B$ at $x$ .
\begin{align*} \Delta t A\left(J_B(x)-J_B(x+\Delta x)\right)+\Delta t \Delta V \sigma_B(x) &= \Delta V \left(c_B(t+\Delta t) - c_B(t)\right) \end{align*}
  • For simplicity, we don't show all variables of all terms:
    $\sigma_B(x) = \sigma_B(x,t),\quad c_B(t)=c_B(x,t),\quad J_B(x)=J_B(x,t)$
  • Dividing both sides by $A \Delta x \Delta t$ (the control volume and time) yields: \begin{align*} -\frac{J_B(x+\Delta x)-J_B(x)}{\Delta x}+\sigma_B(x) &= \frac{c_B(t+\Delta t) - c_B(t)}{\Delta t} \end{align*}
  • Taking the limit as $\Delta x\to 0$ and $\Delta t\to 0$ gives the final result \begin{align*} -\frac{\partial J_B}{\partial x}+\sigma_B &= \frac{\partial c_B}{\partial t} \end{align*}
\begin{align*} -\frac{\partial J_B(x,t)}{\partial x}+\sigma_B &= \frac{\partial c_B(x,t)}{\partial t} \end{align*}
  • We have created a differential balance equation. This equation describes how the concentration of some property $B$ varies in time and space.
  • This is a general equation for any 1D problem and retains all of the properties of the standard balance equation.
  • For example, if there is no accumulation (steady state) and no generation (e.g., for the fluid mass in a pipe) we have \begin{align*} -\frac{\partial J_B(x,t)}{\partial x}+{\cancel{\sigma_B}} &= \cancel{\frac{\partial c_B(x,t)}{\partial t}}\\ \frac{\partial J_B(x,t)}{\partial x} &= 0 &\therefore{J_B = \text{constant}} \end{align*}
  • This is simply a statement that the flow rate of B must remain constant ($\textrm{IN}=\textrm{OUT}$) as the solution to this equation is that $J_B$ is constant.
We wish to extend our previous derivation to three dimensions. First, we must extend our differential volume to three dimensions:
Proceeding as before, balancing all of the fluxes and the generation term, we have: \begin{align*} \Delta x  \Delta y  &\Delta z\left(c_B\left(t+\Delta T\right) - c_B\left(t\right)\right)\\ &= \Delta t \Delta y  \Delta z  J_{B,x}(x,y,z,t) -\Delta t \Delta y  \Delta z  J_{B,x}(x+\Delta x,y,z,t)\\ &+ \Delta t \Delta x \Delta z  J_{B,y}(x,y,z,t) - \Delta t \Delta x  \Delta z  J_{B,y}(x,y+\Delta y,z,t)\\ &+ \Delta t \Delta x \Delta y J_{B,z}(x,y,z,t) - \Delta t \Delta x  \Delta y  J_{B,z}(x,y,z+\Delta z,t)\\ &+ \sigma_B  \Delta x  \Delta y  \Delta z \Delta t \end{align*}
Dividing by the control volume ( $\Delta V=\Delta x  \Delta y  \Delta z$ ) and time ( $\Delta t$ ) we have:
\begin{align*} \frac{c_B(t+\Delta t) - c_B(t)}{\Delta t} = &-\left[\frac{J_{B,x}(x+\Delta x,y,z,t)-J_{B,x}(x,y,z,t)}{\Delta x}\right]\\ &-\left[\frac{J_{B,y}(x,y+\Delta y,z,t)-J_{B,y}(x,y,z,t)}{\Delta y}\right] \\ &-\left[\frac{J_{B,z}(x,y,z+\Delta z,t)-J_{B,z}(x,y,z,t)}{\Delta z}\right] \\ &+\sigma_B \end{align*}
We only need to take the limit as the control volume and time interval shrinks to zero and we will have our differential equation…
  • Taking the limits as $\Delta t\to0$ , $\Delta x \to 0$ , $\Delta y\to 0$ , and $\Delta z \to 0$ we finally have \begin{align*} \frac{\partial c_B}{\partial t} = - \frac{\partial J_{B,x}(x,y,z,t)}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial J_{B,y}(x,y,z,t)}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial J_{B,z}(x,y,z,t)}{\partial z} + \sigma_B \end{align*}
  • This is our 3D general balance equation, but its not in a convenient notation.
  • We can apply our revised knowledge of vectors to express it more cleanly…
This can be written in the vector notation as \begin{align*} \frac{\partial c_B}{\partial t} = -\nabla\cdot \boldsymbol{J}_B + \sigma_B \end{align*} where $\boldsymbol{J}_B=\left\{J_{B,x},J_{B,y},J_{B,z}\right\}$
$\nabla$ (Greek: nabla ) is known as the gradient or del operator, defined in rectangular coordinates as \begin{align*} \nabla = \frac{\partial}{\partial \boldsymbol{r}} = \left\{\frac{\partial}{\partial x}, \frac{\partial}{\partial y}, \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\right\} \end{align*} The position of the control volume is given by the vector $\boldsymbol{r}=\left\{x,y,z\right\}$ .
