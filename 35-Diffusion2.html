Diffusion: Part 2

  • In the previous lecture, we found that for diffusion problems our molar balance equation is \begin{align*} \frac{\partial C_A}{\partial t} = -\nabla\cdot \boldsymbol{N}_A + \sigma_A \end{align*}
  • And it was stated that for small concentrations of $A$ ( $x_A\ll1$ where $x_A$ is the mole fraction of $A$ ) in a binary mixture, the molar flux is given by Fick's law. \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{N}_A = -D_{AB} \nabla C_A \end{align*}
  • But why this restriction to small concentrations?
  • We should revise our definition of diffusion again…
Diffusion is the migration of a single species through another species or material, relative to the bulk motion of the fluid.
  • Diffusion can occur when the fluid is in motion, but its measured relative to the bulk movement of the fluid.
  • But diffusion causes movement of the components of the fluid, so we need to somehow define both bulk movement and diffusion carefully.
  • However, when the diffusing species is negligible ($x_A\ll 1$ ), its movement doesn't really matter, and if its moving through a solid we can assume $\boldsymbol{v}=\boldsymbol{0}$, and use \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{N}_A = -D_{AB} \nabla C_A \end{align*}
  • So we need to ask:
    • How is the bulk motion of the fluid defined?
    • What is the full definition of$N_A$?
  • When there is diffusion, each species in a mixture will move with its own velocity, $\boldsymbol{v}_A$.
  • We can then define the bulk motion in two ways, we can either take the mass or molar average velocity \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{v}_{mass-avg.} &= \frac{1}{\rho}\sum_A \rho_A  \boldsymbol{v}_A & \boldsymbol{v}_{molar-avg.} &= \sum_A x_A \boldsymbol{v}_A \end{align*}
  • In the continuity, momentum and energy balances we've derived, $\boldsymbol{v}=\boldsymbol{v}_{mass-avg.}$.
  • But we've been working with molar fluxes for diffusion so far ($N_A$ ), so we'll keep using molar-fluxes/fractions/concentrations.
  • But you should know that this treatment can also be done in mass fluxes/fractions/concentrations and you will obtain identical results.
  • C&R vol. 1 starts with molar and generalises to mass fluxes.
  • Transport Phenomena starts with mass fluxes and generalises to molar, but has a section on both and how they are equivalent (2nd Ed., Sec. 17.7).
  • If each species moves with a velocity $\boldsymbol{v}_A$, the molar flux is given by $\boldsymbol{N}_A = C_A \boldsymbol{v}_A$
  • The diffusive flux is defined as the motion of the species relative to the bulk velocity, which gives us this definition \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{J}_A=C_A \left(\boldsymbol{v}_A-\boldsymbol{v}_{molar-avg.}\right) \end{align*}
  • Using these two equations and the definition of $\boldsymbol{v}_{molar-avg.}=\sum_A x_A \boldsymbol{v}_A$, we can write \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{N}_A &= C_A \left(\boldsymbol{v}_A-\boldsymbol{v}_{molar-avg.}\right) + C_A \boldsymbol{v}_{molar-avg.}\\ &=\boldsymbol{J}_A + C_A \sum_B x_B \boldsymbol{v}_B\\ &=\boldsymbol{J}_A + x_A \sum_B C_B \boldsymbol{v}_B\\ \boldsymbol{N}_A &=\boldsymbol{J}_A + x_A \sum_B \boldsymbol{N}_B \end{align*}
  • This is our final, general expression for the flux $\boldsymbol{N}_A$, where $\boldsymbol{J}_A$ is our diffusive flux.
  • In the special (but common) case of a binary mixture, we have \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{N}_A &=\boldsymbol{J}_A + x_A \left(\boldsymbol{N}_A + \boldsymbol{N}_B\right) \end{align*}
  • Provided you have an ideal mixture, the diffusive flux is given by the molar form of Fick's law. \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{J}_A=-D_{AB} \nabla C_A \end{align*}
  • Which gives \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{N}_A &=-D_{AB} \nabla C_A + x_A \left(\boldsymbol{N}_A + \boldsymbol{N}_B\right) \end{align*}
  • This is the equation you can use to solve all problems in the next tutorial.
  • For example, at small concentrations we have $x_A\to0$ which gives Ficks law back as the solution for the flux, \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{N}_A &=-D_{AB} \nabla C_A + \cancelto{0}{x_A} \left(\boldsymbol{N}_A + \boldsymbol{N}_B\right). \end{align*}
  • In solids, the maximum absorbed concentration of a gas or liquid is usually very low compared to the solid density.
  • Therefore, assuming the concentration of diffusing species is low ($x_A\to0$) is a good assumption when you are treating diffusion through solids.
  • The hydrogen diffusion tutorial question (see right) and the previous class example are both applications of this assumption.
  • If you have diffusion in a binary ideal gas with constant pressure (no flow) and temperature (no conduction), there must be something called equimolar counterdiffusion.
  • In this case, when one mole of $A$ diffuses in one direction, one mole of $B$ must diffuse in the opposite direction in order to maintain the constant pressure, $N_A = -N_B$.
  • The expression for the flux then becomes \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{N}_A &=-D_{AB} \nabla C_A + x_A \left(\boldsymbol{N}_A + \boldsymbol{N}_B\right)\\ &=-D_{AB} \nabla C_A + x_A \left(\cancelto{0}{\boldsymbol{N}_A - \boldsymbol{N}_A}\right) \end{align*}
  • The tutorial has an example of equimolar counterdiffusion, when you have diffusion between two different concentrations of gas (see right). This also applies in distillation columns where you have Constant Molar Overflow.
  • Finally, we consider the case where one species is stationary ($N_B=0$).
  • This may occur when the other species, $A$, is being absorbed, generated or transported away but $B$ isnt.
  • The expression for the flux then becomes \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{N}_A &=-D_{AB} \nabla C_A + x_A \left(\boldsymbol{N}_A + \cancelto{0}{\boldsymbol{N}_B}\right)\\ \boldsymbol{N}_A &=-\frac{D_{AB}}{1-x_A} \nabla C_A \end{align*}
  • This is known as Stefan's Law, and shows that in binary diffusion, when one species is stationary there is an enhancement of the diffusion of the other species.
  • The Winklemann experiment (see right), used to determine values of the diffusivity is an example of this, and other examples are available in the tutorial questions.
  • When we needed to compare competing momentum and energy transports processes, we did this through a range of dimensionless parameters: $\text{Re}$, $\text{Pr}$, $\text{Nu}$, $\text{Gr}$, $\text{Ra}$.
  • Introducing mass transfer allows us to define two more.
  • The Schmidt number, $\text{Sc}$, is the ratio of the momentum and diffusive transport processes in the system. \begin{align*} \text{Sc} = \frac{\nu}{D_{AB}} = \frac{\mu}{\rho D_{AB}} \end{align*}
  • The Lewis number is defined as the ratio of the conductive and diffusive transport processes in the system. \begin{align*} \text{Le} = \frac{\alpha}{D_{AB}} = \frac{k}{\rho C_p D_{AB}} \end{align*}
  • These parameters are key to characterising systems such as boiling or combustion, where mass, momentum and heat transfer are combined and occurring simultaneously. It should be clear though, that whenever we add new forms of transport (e.g., electrical/magnetic), there will be a set of additional dimensionless numbers taking the ratio of their phenomenological coefficients.
