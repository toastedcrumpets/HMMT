  • Chemical engineers are often called “glorified plumbers,” one of the largest concerns in the petroleum industries today is flow assurance, and, somewhat flippantly, we can say that pipe-flow is the fundamental unit-operation.
  • Whenever we pump liquids or gases or both through pipes, we need to know what the pressure loss is for specified flow properties (i.e., pressure drop, diameter, density, viscosity).
  • We also need to know about Reynolds numbers and the onset of turbulent flow.
  • Today we will derive the equation used to describe laminar pipe-flow, the Hagen-Poiseuille equation.

  • Let's take the simplest example of pipe-flow, such as steady-state , laminar , and incompressible flow in a pipe.
  • Following the last lectures discussion on symmetry , we should place our cylindrical coordinate system so that the $\hat{\boldsymbol{z}}$ axis is aligned with the axis of symmetry (see figure).
  • We can then immediately see that our first boundary condition is the no-slip condition at the pipe wall ($r=R$ ): \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{v}(r=R) &= \boldsymbol{0} \end{align*}
  • This is much more convenient than trying to define it in Cartesian coordinates.
  • The second boundary condition arises from the axis of symmetry in the centre of the pipe. Here the stresses in the $r$ and $\theta$ directions are zero. \begin{align*} \tau_{ij}(r=0) &= 0 & \text{where $i$,$j\in[r,\theta] $} \end{align*}
  • If the flow doesn't vary in the $\hat{\boldsymbol{z}}$ direction (we will prove it doesn't in a moment), then all stresses will be zero on the axis of symmetry \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{\tau}(r=0) &= \boldsymbol{0} \end{align*}
  • Starting with the continuity equation, if we assume the flow is incompressible then the density is constant and we have the standard result: \begin{align*} \cancelto{0}{\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t}} - \nabla \cdot \rho\boldsymbol{v} &= 0\\ \nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{v} &= 0 \end{align*}
  • As we're in cylindrical coordinates, we must look up the result of the gradient operator in cylindrical coordinates: \begin{align*} \nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{v} &=\frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial  r v_r}{\partial r} + \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial  v_\theta}{\partial \theta} + \frac{\partial  v_z}{\partial z} \end{align*}
  • We can then use this in the equation above to get the mass balance for our current system: \begin{align*} \frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial  r v_r}{\partial r} + \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial  v_\theta}{\partial \theta} + \frac{\partial  v_z}{\partial z} = 0 \end{align*}
  • In the previous examples, we deleted most of the terms by assuming that the flow velocity is only non-zero in one direction. What is the reasoning behind this?
  • If we assume that the flow is laminar, what else do we have to assume to say that $v_\theta=0$ , $v_r=0$ ?
  • If a flow is laminar, this implies that viscous forces dominate the flow.
  • This means that, if there is no unapposed driving-force in a direction, eventually the flow will slow to zero.
  • Lets look at an example of developing flow …
  • Consider pressure-driven flow entering a long pipeline.
  • At first, the inertia of the flow causes the flow to appear turbulent, with flow in all directions.
  • After a sufficient distance, viscous forces dampen the flow until it reaches the well-developed flow profile.
  • If the flow was turbulent, interial forces are always stronger than viscous forces and the flow never settles, but with laminar flow we can expect a well-developed flow profile to appear.
  • So, if this flow is laminar, and we're far enough from the pipe entry, we can assume it is well-developed flow.
  • This implies that $v_\theta=0$ and $v_r=0$ , taking another look at the continuity equation we have: \begin{align*} \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial r \cancelto{0}{v_r}}{\partial r}+ \frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial \cancelto{0}{v_\theta}}{\partial \theta} + \frac{\partial v_z}{\partial z} &=0\\ \frac{\partial v_z}{\partial z} &= 0 \end{align*}
  • Again, we have that if we assume the flow is incompressible , laminar and well-developed , we find the velocity profile will not vary along the pipe.
  • In summary, we have the following boundary conditions: \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{v}(r=R) &= \boldsymbol{0} & \boldsymbol{\tau}(r=0) &= \boldsymbol{0} \end{align*}
  • As the flow is laminar and well-developed , the flow perpendicular to the axis is zero: \begin{align*} v_r &= 0 & v_\theta &= 0 \end{align*}
  • Although the axial flow velocity is non-zero, $v_z\neq 0$ , we know from the continuity equation its $z$ -derivative is zero: \begin{align*} \frac{\partial v_z}{\partial z} = 0 \end{align*}
  • Lets solve the Cauchy momentum equation for the stress profile…
  • Starting with the full Cauchy momentum equation: \begin{align*} \rho\frac{\partial\boldsymbol{v}}{\partial t} &= -\rho\boldsymbol{v}\cdot \nabla\boldsymbol{v} - \nabla\cdot\boldsymbol{\tau} - \nabla p + \rho \boldsymbol{g} \end{align*}
  • If the flow is steady state, the time derivate is zero and we have: \begin{align*} \rho \boldsymbol{v}\cdot\nabla \boldsymbol{v} = -\nabla\cdot\boldsymbol{\tau} - \nabla p + \rho \boldsymbol{g} \end{align*}
  • As the flow is well developed we know we're only interested in $\boldsymbol{v}_z$ (the other dimensions are either hydrostatic head or a statement that certain stresses are zero).
  • Selecting the $z$ component, we have \begin{align*} \rho[\boldsymbol{v}\cdot\nabla \boldsymbol{v}]_z = -[\nabla\cdot\boldsymbol{\tau}]_z - [\nabla p]_z + \rho g_z \end{align*}
  • Looking up these terms one by one, we have: \begin{align*} [\boldsymbol{v}\cdot\nabla \boldsymbol{v}]_z = v_r \frac{\partial v_z}{\partial r} + \frac{v_\theta}{r}\frac{\partial v_z}{\partial \theta} + v_z \frac{\partial v_z}{\partial z} \end{align*}
  • But we have $v_r=v_\theta=0$ , and the continuity equation states $\frac{\partial v_z}{\partial z} = 0$ so this whole term is zero, giving us: \begin{align*} -[\nabla\cdot\boldsymbol{\tau}]_z = [\nabla p]_z - \rho g_z \end{align*}
\begin{align*} -[\nabla\cdot\boldsymbol{\tau}]_z = [\nabla p]_z - \rho g_z \end{align*}
  • Which terms of the stress are non-zero? Expanding we have: \begin{align} \left[\nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{\tau}\right]_z &= \frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial }{\partial r}\left(r \tau_{rz}\right) + \frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial \tau_{\theta z}}{\partial\theta} + \frac{\partial  \tau_{z z}}{\partial z} \end{align}
  • We know that $\partial/\partial \theta$ is zero as the problem is rotationally symmetric in $\theta$ (no transport in the direction of symmetry, so $\nabla_\theta v_z = 0$ ). We also know that $\tau_{zz}$ is zero as the velocity does not change in the $z$ direction ( $\partial v_z/\partial z=0$ ).
  • Therefore, the only non-zero element of the stress term is when $i=r$ , leaving us with the final equation: \begin{align*} -\frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial }{\partial r}\left(r \tau_{rz}\right) = \frac{\partial p}{\partial z} - \rho g_z \end{align*}
\begin{align*} -\frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial }{\partial r}\left(r \tau_{rz}\right) = \frac{\partial p}{\partial z} - \rho g_z \end{align*}
  • First, we note that the stress, gravity, and density are independent of the pressure or $z$ component. This means we can perform the integration over $p$ and $z$ , treating everything else as a constant term. \begin{align*} \frac{\partial p}{\partial z} &= -\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial  r \tau_{rz}}{\partial r} + \rho g_z\\ \int_{p_0}^{p_L}{\rm d}p &= \int^L_0 \left(-\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial  r \tau_{rz}}{\partial r} + \rho g_z \right){\rm d z}\\ -\frac{1}{r} \frac{\partial  r \tau_{rz}}{\partial r} &= \frac{\Delta p}{L} - \rho g_z \end{align*} where $L$ is the pipe length and $\Delta p=p_L-p_0$ is the pressure drop over the length of the pipe.
  • We can perform the integration with respect to $r$ to yield: \begin{align*} \tau_{rz} = \left(\rho g_z - \frac{\Delta p}{L}\right)\frac{r}{2} + \frac{C}{r} \end{align*}
\begin{align*} \tau_{rz} = \left(\rho g_z - \frac{\Delta p}{L}\right)\frac{r}{2} + \frac{C}{r} \end{align*}
  • The symmetry boundary condition and incompressible nature of the flow causes all stresses to be zero in the middle of the pipe $\boldsymbol{\tau}(r=0)=\boldsymbol{0}$ .
  • But regardless of the boundary condition, we can see that we must set $C=0$ to simply have a finite stress in the middle of the pipe!
  • We don't actually need the symmetry boundary condition to see this, but its a reinforcement of our decision to set $C=0$ . Giving us the stress profile in the pipe: \begin{align*} \tau_{rz} = \left(\rho g_z - \frac{\Delta p}{L}\right)\frac{r}{2} \end{align*}
  • We can now calculate the velocity profile in the pipe…
\begin{align*} \tau_{rz} = \left(\rho g_z - \frac{\Delta p}{L}\right)\frac{r}{2} \end{align*}
  • Looking up the definition of the stress in our table, we have: \begin{align*} \tau_{rz} = \tau_{zr} = -\mu\left(\frac{\partial v_r}{\partial z} + \frac{\partial v_z}{\partial r}\right) \end{align*}
  • As $v_r=0$ , this simplifies, and inserting it into the stress balance (top) we have: \begin{align*} \tau_{rz} = -\mu \frac{\partial v_z}{\partial r} = \left(\rho g_z-\frac{\Delta p}{L}\right)\frac{r}{2} \end{align*}
\begin{align*} -\mu \frac{\partial v_z}{\partial r} = \left(\rho g_z-\frac{\Delta p}{L}\right)\frac{r}{2} \end{align*}
  • Performing the integration, we have \begin{align*} v_z = \left(\frac{\Delta p}{L}-\rho g_z\right)\frac{r^2}{4 \mu} + C \end{align*}
  • As the velocity on the wall is zero, $v_z(r=R)=0$ , we can determine the constant and write \begin{align*} v_z = \left(\frac{-\Delta p}{L} + \rho g_z\right)\frac{R^2 - r^2}{4 \mu} \end{align*}
\begin{align*} v_z = \left(\frac{-\Delta p}{L} + \rho g_z\right)\frac{R^2 - r^2}{4 \mu} \end{align*}
  • We can now determine interesting properties, such as the maximum velocity \begin{align*} v_z^{max} = \left(\frac{-\Delta p}{L} + \rho g_z\right)\frac{R^2}{4 \mu} \end{align*}
  • Or the volumetric flow \begin{align*} \dot{V}_z &= \int_0^R\int_0^{2\pi} v_z  r {\rm d}\theta  {\rm d}r\\ &= \pi\left(\frac{-\Delta p}{L} + \rho g_z\right)\frac{R^4}{8 \mu} \end{align*} (the extra $r$ factor appears as it is the differential area of the $\theta r$ surface, $A_{\theta r}=r \delta\theta \delta r$ ).
  • This is a famous expression known as the Hagen-Poiseuille equation. It relates the flow rate to the pressure drop in a pipe for laminar , well-developed , Newtonian flows.
