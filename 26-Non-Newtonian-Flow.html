Non-Newtonian Flows

  • We have learnt how to use the balance equations in rectangular, cylindrical and spherical geometries. \begin{align*} \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} &=- \nabla\cdot \rho \boldsymbol{v}\\ \rho\frac{\partial \boldsymbol{v}}{\partial t} &= -\rho \boldsymbol{v}\cdot\nabla \boldsymbol{v} - \nabla\cdot\boldsymbol{\tau} - \nabla p + \rho \boldsymbol{g}\\ \rho C_p\frac{\partial T}{\partial t} &= -\rho C_p  v_j  \nabla_j T - \nabla_i q_i - \tau_{ji} \nabla_j v_i - p \nabla_i v_i +\sigma_{energy} \end{align*}
  • We also looked at the flow of Newtonian fluids in a various geometries and derived the Hagen-Poiseuille equation. \begin{align*} \dot{V}_z &= \pi\left(\frac{-\Delta p}{L} + \rho g_z\right)\frac{R^4}{8 \mu} \end{align*}
  • This is an important design equation for calculating (laminar) pressure drop in pipes.
  • We will now explore how Newtonian and non-Newtonian fluids differ.
  • Typically, low-molecular weight fluids such as water, molten metals and gases exhibit Newtonian behaviour.
  • In a Newtonian fluid at constant temperature and pressure the stress is related by Newton's “law” \begin{align*} \tau_{xy} = -\mu \frac{\partial v_x}{\partial y} \end{align*} ( Note: This does not include bulk terms that appear for $\tau_{xx}$, $\tau_{yy}$, and $\tau_{zz}$ when the fluid is compressible).
  • This is the definition of a Newtonian fluid, the stress obey's the law above; However, there are a wide range of fluids which exhibit non-Newtonian behaviour: Adhesives, ales, dough, blood, butter, chocolate, cement, cosmetics, custard, mud, grease, waxy crude oils, polymer melts, sewerage, toothpaste, … … are all examples of non-Newtonian fluids!
  • How does their behaviour differ from Newtonian fluids?
  • On the left we have the shear stress ( $\tau_{xy}$ ) versus shear rate ( $\frac{\partial v_x}{\partial y}$ ) for three of the most common models for non-Newtonian fluids.
  • The baseline system for comparison is the Newtonian fluid, which has a linear relationship between the stress and strain. \begin{align*} \tau_{xy} = -\mu \frac{\partial v_x}{\partial y} \end{align*}
  • For the Newtonian fluid, the slope of the plot is the viscosity…
  • … for other fluid model types, the slope of the line, connecting a point on the model back to the origin, is the “apparent” viscosity. More later…
  • The most common form of non-Newtonian fluids are Shear-thinning or Pseudoplastic fluids. Here the apparent viscosity decreases as more stress is applied.
  • Examples of these fluids include polymer melts and solids suspensions.
  • When the fluid is sheared, structural changes occur which reduce the viscosity of the fluid. For example, in polymer melts the polymers are “combed” and align along the direction of shear.
  • This is just like long knotted hair. As you comb/shear it, it becomes easier to comb/shear.
  • Shear thickening or dilatant fluids are not as common as shear thinning fluids but their behaviour is very dramatic.
  • As the fluid is sheared the viscosity increases and many shear-thickening fluids can even turn solid.
  • Corn starch or uncooked custard powder with a small amount of water is a very popular shear thickening fluid.
    See the swimming pool experiment: {http://youtu.be/f2XQ97XHjVw} Or what happens if you shake it: {http://youtu.be/3zoTKXXNQIU} Or how you can “pump” it with a wheel (see video).
  • Finally, we concern ourselves with fluids with a yield stress, $\tau_0$, known as Viscoplastics.
  • These fluids will not flow until the stress in the fluid reaches the yield stress. After this the fluid may be shear-thinning, shear-thickening, or Newtonian.
  • The simplest model of a viscoplastic material is the Bingham plastic model, which has a Newtonian response above the yield stress.
  • Examples of viscoplastic fluids are blood, ketchup/red sauce, toothpaste, and mayonnaise. Many suspensions or gels, such as paint or jelly are examples of viscoplastic fluids.
  • If we want to model non-Newtonian flows, we need to select an expression which closely approximates the real stress/strain relationship.
  • We can then use this expression in the Cauchy momentum equation to derive the velocity profile, then a Non-Newtonian equilivent to the Hagen-Poiseuille equation.
  • To try to develop a model for Non-newtonian fluids, let us consider if we were testing the viscous behaviour of a new fluid.
  • We could place the fluid between two moveable plates (see tutorial 2) and shear it at a fixed shear-rate ($\dot{\gamma}=\left|\frac{\partial v_x}{\partial y}\right|$ ).
  • We could then measure the stress $\left|\tau_{xy}\right|$ (which is related to the power) required to maintain this shear rate.
  • We can then use Newton's law to calculate the apparent viscosity of the fluid \begin{align*} \frac{\left|\tau_{xy}\right| }{\dot{\gamma}} = \mu_{app.} \end{align*}
  • Let's look at the apparent viscosity of a real fluid…
Coulson & Richardson, “Volume 1: Fluid Flow, Heat Transfer and Mass Transfer,” 6th Ed., Elsevier
  • At low shear rates the apparent viscosity tends towards a limiting value of $\mu_{app.}\to\mu_0$.
  • At high shear rates the apparent viscosity tends towards a limiting value of $\mu_{app.}\to\mu_{\infty}$.
  • However, in the middle of the plot the apparent viscosity appears to be relatively linear…
  • A straight line on a log-log plot suggests that a power-law is a suitable function to fit. \begin{align*} y &= m x^n \\ \ln y &= n\ln x +\ln m \end{align*} (the slope of the $\ln y$ vs $\ln x$ plot is the power: $n$ )
  • A simple model for both shear thickening and shear thinning fluids is then the Power Law fluid. The shear stress for a Power Law fluid is given by \begin{align*} \left|\tau_{xy}\right| = k \left|\frac{\partial v_x}{\partial y}\right|^n \end{align*} where $k$ is known as the consistency coefficient. This is also called the Ostwald-de Waele relationship.
  • Note that this is always positive. If you want to obtain the correct sign of the stress, you need to make sure the stress opposes the velocity gradient like so: \begin{align*} \tau_{xy} = - k \left|\frac{\partial v_x}{\partial y}\right|^{n-1} \frac{\partial v_x}{\partial y} \end{align*}
  • Although you may be tempted to call $k$ the “viscosity”, you should note that the units of $k$ change as a function of $n$, so we can't directly compare the consistency coefficients of different fluids (except when their $n$ values are equal).
    • When $n=1$, the flow is Newtonian and $k=\mu$.
    • For $n>1$, the fluid becomes thicker as the shear rate is increased.
    • For $n<1$, the fluid becomes thinner as the shear rate is increased.
\begin{align*} \left|\tau_{xy}\right| = -k \left|\frac{\partial v_x}{\partial y}\right|^n \end{align*}
  • Let's define the apparent viscosity as the viscosity Newton's law needs to have to give you the same stress at the current shear rate.
  • Setting the power law stress equal to the stress from Newton's law and rearranging for the viscosity, we have: \begin{align*} \left|\tau_{xy}\right|&= k \left|\frac{\partial v_x}{\partial y}\right|^{n} =\mu_{app.}\left|\frac{\partial v_x}{\partial y}\right| \\ \mu_{app.}&= k \left|\frac{\partial v_x}{\partial y}\right|^{n-1} \end{align*}
  • This expression gives the apparent viscosity as a function of shear rate. It is the viscosity needed in Newton's law to give you the same stress as the fluid is giving, i.e., it is the viscosity the fluid appears to have.
Coulson & Richardson, “Volume 1: Fluid Flow, Heat Transfer and Mass Transfer,” 6th Ed., Elsevier
\begin{align*} \mu_{app.}&= k \left|\frac{\partial v_x}{\partial y}\right|^{n-1} \end{align*}
  • But there's a problem with the Power Law model with its limiting behaviour.
  • Taking $n<1$ for a shear thinning fluid:
    • As the shear rate tends to zero, the viscosity tends to infinity, not $\mu_0$ !
    • When the shear rate tends to infinity, the viscosity tends to zero, not $\mu_\infty$ !
  • Power law fluids only work over a specified range of behaviour. Luckily, this range is usually quite big and encompasses the industrially interesting range of shear rates.
  • Just be careful with creeping or slow flows of fluids!
  • You need to be aware of this limitation, but there are other models available which “fix” these problems. For example the Reiner-Philippoff Model \begin{align*} -\frac{\partial v_x}{\partial y} = \frac{\tau_{xy}}{\mu_\infty + \frac{\mu_0-\mu_\infty}{1+\left(\tau_{xy}/\tau_{s}\right)^2}} \end{align*} which has the correct limits at zero and infinite shear, along with an additional fitting parameter $\tau_s$.
  • We will not cover these models, but you can look them up in standard texts such as the Transport Phenomena book.
  • We will only consider the Power-Law model for shear-thinning and shear thickening fluids, as it is sufficient to introduce all of the fundamentals of these flows.
  • But we still need a model with a yield stress…
  • Viscoplastic materials only flow above a certain yield stress $\tau_0$ (they usually just start to yield a little before this, its not a sharp change in real life; However, a sharp change is theoretically easier to calculate with).
  • Once they yield, they may have any behaviour, such as a power law fluid.
  • The Bingham plastic is a simple model where the fluid is Newtonian above the yield stress. \begin{align*} \frac{\partial v_x}{\partial y} = \begin{cases} -\mu^{-1}\left(\tau_{xy}-\tau_0)\right) & \text{if $\tau_{xy} > \tau_0 $} \\ 0 & \text{if $\tau_{xy}\leq\tau_0 $} \end{cases} \end{align*}
  • This leads to unusual flows: For example, in a pipe flow of a Bingham Plastic the centre of the flow is a solid with a liquid surrounding it in the higher-stress areas.
  • We have seen that for steady-state incompressible laminar flows the momentum equation often reduces to \begin{align*} \nabla\cdot\boldsymbol{\tau} = \rho \boldsymbol{g} - \nabla p \end{align*}
  • This equation can typically be integrated to determine the stress profile $\boldsymbol{\tau}$ of the flow without reference to the viscosity of the fluid.
  • For a given flow configuration and pressure drop the steady state stress profile is the same for all types of fluids.
  • As the form of the above equation suggests, the stress profile is typically linear in the position for rectangular systems.
  • A minimum in the stress will appear at points, lines or planes of symmetry and a maximum of stress always appears at the wall boundaries (if the fluid is in motion).
\begin{align*} \tau_{xy} &= \frac{p_{out}-p_{in}}{\mu  L}\left(y - \frac{H}{2}\right) \end{align*} For flow between two plates we have a nice linear stress profile, with zero stress in the middle of the flow.
\begin{align*} \tau_{rz} = \left(\frac{\Delta p}{L} - \rho g_z\right)\frac{r}{2} \end{align*} With flow in a pipe, we again have a nice linear stress profile, with zero stress in the middle of the flow. In general, the stress always peaks at wall boundaries in the system, it also has the same value at these walls.
\begin{align*} \tau_{xy} &= \rho g_x\left(Y - y\right) \end{align*} Again, another linear profile of the stress, peaking at the wall, but with a zero value at the free surface. This solution is the bottom half of gravity-driven flow between two plates.
Stress can be nonlinear too: \begin{align*} \tau_{rz} =-\frac{\rho g_z}{2}\left(r-\frac{\lambda^2 R^2}{r}\right) \end{align*} For film flow around the outside of a pipe, the stress has a nonlinear form. But the stress still peaks at the no-slip boundaries.
A similar story appears for the annular flow (see tutorial 3): \begin{align*} \tau_{rz} &= \frac{1}{2}\left(\rho g_z - \frac{\Delta p}{L}\right)\left(r - \frac{\lambda^2  R_0^2}{r}\right) \end{align*} The stress again peaks at the boundaries and is nonlinear. Why does the stress change sign for the annulus, but not for the pipe?
  • It is crucial to note, regardless of the geometry, we can solve for the stress profile without knowing the rheological behaviour of the fluid.
  • The stress profile is the solution of the Cauchy momentum equation, it is only when we want to derive the velocity profile that we actually need a Newton's law/Power law/Bingham model.
  • This invariance of the stress profile allows us, if we're careful, to derive the stress profile just once for a certain geometry.
  • We can then calculate the velocity profiles and flow rates of the flows for Newtonian and Non-Newtonian flows straight from the stress profile.
  • We'll look at this through the following, motivating example:

Example

  • You're a member of BeeP, a huge multinational company who have set up an office in America.
  • The CEO has a great plan to save money, the environment and their image. Instead of shipping oil around the world, they think it would be easier and cheaper to just pipe it everywhere.
  • The business development division is tasked with making sure great new ideas like this are viable, so they go straight to their library to look up a rough sizing calculation for pipelines.
  • Unfortunately they found that a visitor from their gulf-of-mexico office has spilt oil all over their copy of Perrys, C&R, and Campbells!
  • They need an expression for the pressure drop for a given flow rate, pipe length, and radius. But here's the kicker, the oil they want to pipe is non-Newtonian, so they can't just use the Hagen-Poiseuille equation anymore! \begin{align*} \cancel{\dot{V}_z = \pi\left(\frac{-\Delta p}{L} + \rho g_z\right)\frac{R^4}{8 \mu}} \end{align*}
  • Luckily, the one of the top engineers in the business development unit attended EG3030 at Aberdeen.
  • Of course they remembered that the stress profile in a cylindrical pipe is always the same, regardless of the type of fluid. They even remembered the expression for the stress in a pipe \begin{align*} \tau_{rz} = \left(\rho g_z - \frac{\Delta p}{L}\right)\frac{r}{2} \end{align*}
  • Using equally superhuman feats of recollection, the engineer also remembered the Power Law model and that it works pretty well for non-Newtonian flows. \begin{align*} \left|\tau_{rz}\right| = k \left|\frac{\partial v_z}{\partial r}\right|^n \end{align*}
  • If the engineer can somehow connect these two, they can work out the velocity profile in the pipe. They can then integrate this to obtain the volumetric flow, an improved Hagen-Poiseuille-like expression, the respect of their colleagues, and a large bonus in the process.
  • By rearranging the Power Law model to make the shear rate the subject \begin{align*} \left|\frac{\partial v_z}{\partial r}\right| = \left|\frac{\tau_{rz}}{k}\right|^{\frac{1}{n}} \end{align*}
  • Noting that $r$ is always positive, the expression for the shear stress in a pipe can be substituted in \begin{align*} \left|\frac{\partial v_z}{\partial r}\right| = \left|\frac{\Delta p}{2 k L} - \frac{\rho \boldsymbol{g}_z}{2 k}\right|^{\frac{1}{n}}r^{\frac{1}{n}} \end{align*}
  • The engineer can now solve the equation by integration… \begin{align*} \int {\rm d} \left|v_z\right| = \int \left|\frac{\Delta p}{2 k L} - \frac{\rho \boldsymbol{g}_z}{2 k}\right|^{\frac{1}{n}}r^{\frac{1}{n}}  {\rm d} r \end{align*}
  • Noting that $1/n + 1 = (n+1)/n$, we obtain \begin{align*} \left|v_z\right| = \left|\frac{\Delta p}{2 k L} - \frac{\rho \boldsymbol{g}_z}{2 k}\right|^{\frac{1}{n}}\frac{n}{n+1}r^{\frac{n+1}{n}}+C \end{align*}
  • Even though the expression is starting to look a little grim, the engineer perseveres and attempts to figure out the constant of integration \begin{align*} \left|v_z\right| = \left|\frac{\Delta p}{2 k L} - \frac{\rho \boldsymbol{g}_z}{2 k}\right|^{\frac{1}{n}}\frac{n}{n+1}r^{\frac{n+1}{n}}+C \end{align*}
  • Remembering that boundary conditions are useful for solving for constants, the engineer assumes that at the pipe wall they will have the no-slip condition $v_z(r=R)=0$. Setting $r=R$ and $v_z=0$ in their last equation, the engineer obtains an expression for the constant term. \begin{align*} C = -\left|\frac{\Delta p}{2 k L} - \frac{\rho \boldsymbol{g}_z}{2 k}\right|^{\frac{1}{n}}\frac{n}{n+1}R^{\frac{n+1}{n}} \end{align*}
  • As $r \le R$, and substituting this back in allows an expression for the flow profile to be calculated. \begin{align*} \left|v_z\right| = \left|\frac{\Delta p}{2 k L} - \frac{\rho \boldsymbol{g}_z}{2 k}\right|^{\frac{1}{n}}\frac{n}{n+1}\left(R^{\frac{n+1}{n}} - r^{\frac{n+1}{n}}\right) \end{align*}
\begin{align*} \left|v_z\right| = \left|\frac{\Delta p}{2 k L} - \frac{\rho \boldsymbol{g}_z}{2 k}\right|^{\frac{1}{n}}\frac{n}{n+1}\left(R^{\frac{n+1}{n}} - r^{\frac{n+1}{n}}\right) \end{align*}
  • The Engineer isn't done yet, they need something like the Hagen-Poiseuille equation, relating the volumetric flow rate, pressure drop and pipeline size.
  • But the Engineer decides to try and simplify the equation a little first.
  • The maximum velocity in the pipe occurs at the centre line of the pipe. This velocity is found by taking the equation for the flow velocity and setting $r=0$ \begin{align*} \left|v_z^{max}\right| = \left|\frac{\Delta p}{2 k L} - \frac{\rho \boldsymbol{g}_z}{2 k}\right|^{\frac{1}{n}}\frac{n}{n+1}R^{\frac{n+1}{n}} \end{align*}
  • This can be used to rewrite the flow velocity in terms of the maximum velocity \begin{align*} v_z = v_z^{max}\left(1 - \left(\frac{r}{R}\right)^{\frac{n+1}{n}}\right) \end{align*}
\begin{align*} v_z = v_z^{max}\left(1 - \left(\frac{r}{R}\right)^{\frac{n+1}{n}}\right) \end{align*}
  • Going for the finish line, the engineer remembers that volumetric flow-rate is related to the velocity by an integral over the cross-sectional area of the pipe \begin{align*} \dot{V}_z=\int_0^R\int_0^{2\pi}v_z r {\rm d}\theta {\rm d}r \end{align*}
  • The $\theta$ integral can be performed immediately as $v_z$ does not depend on $\theta$. \begin{align*} \dot{V}_z=2 \pi\int_0^Rv_z  r {\rm d}r \end{align*}
  • Substituting in the flow velocity gives \begin{align*} \dot{V}_z=2 \pi v_z^{max} \int_0^R\left(1 - \left(\frac{r}{R}\right)^{\frac{n+1}{n}}\right) r {\rm d}r \end{align*}
\begin{align*} \dot{V}_z=2 \pi v_z^{max} \int_0^R\left(1 - \left(\frac{r}{R}\right)^{\frac{n+1}{n}}\right) r {\rm d}r \end{align*}
  • Finishing the integration gives \begin{align*} \dot{V}_z&=2 \pi v_z^{max} \left[\frac{r^2}{2} - \frac{n}{3n+1}\frac{r^{\frac{3n+1}{n}}}{R^{\frac{n+1}{n}}}\right]^R_0\\ &= 2 \pi R^2 v_z^{max} \left(\frac{1}{2} - \frac{n}{3n+1}\right)\\ &= \pi R^2 v_z^{max} \frac{n+1}{3n+1} \end{align*}
  • Job done. The engineer has a design equation for non-Newtonian fluid flow in a pipeline.
  • The engineer then proves that the CEO's plan is a terrible one as with very long pipes ($L>>1$ ), it would require either huge pressure drops or enormous pipe radii to achieve even a low volumetric flow rate ( $v_z^{max}\propto \Delta P / L$ ).
  • But for some reason the CEO wouldn't give the engineer his bonus…
  • Maybe it was because the Engineer forgot to check if the flow was turbulent first?
