Dimensional Analysis

Enrico Fermi estimated the yield of the Trinity nuclear test by dropping paper during the shockwave and measuring the displacement.
G.I. Taylor needed only a photograph and dimensional analysis, let's learn this power.

Review

  • In the previous lecture, we revised the general heat flux equation and that resistances may be summed. \begin{align*} Q &= U A \Delta T = \frac{\Delta T}{R_{total}}\\ R_{total} &= R_1 + R_2 + … = 1/U_1 A_1 + 1/U_2 A_2 + … \end{align*}
  • We covered conduction and derived the conductive resistances in a plate/pipe from the heat equation. \begin{align*} R_{plate} &= \frac{X}{k A}& R_{pipe} &= \frac{\ln\left(\frac{R_o}{R_i}\right)}{2 \pi L k} \end{align*}
  • We also covered forced convection, and revised that we have relationships for the heat transfer coefficient $h$ \begin{align*} h&=\frac{k \text{Nu}}{L} & \text{Nu} &\approx C \text{Re}^m \text{Pr}^n \end{align*}
  • This expression for $\text{Nu}$ is in the form of a result derived from von Kármán boundary layer analysis, which is too advanced to cover for this course.
  • But this expression contains dimensionless numbers which keep appearing in our lectures, is there something more to them than meets the eye?

Motivation for dimensional analysis

  • You should already be interested in dimensional analysis from the work we've already carried out.
  • For example, we've found that when we're integrating it can be really convenient to perform a change of variables into dimensionless variables first.
  • For example, the volumetric flow through an annulus required us to integrate this equation, which was a lot easier in the dimensionless variable $x=r/R$ : \begin{align*} v_z &= -\frac{\Delta p R^2}{4 L \mu}\left(\frac{r^2}{R^2} - \frac{\kappa^2-1}{\log\kappa}\log \left(\frac{r}{R}\right) -1\right) \end{align*}
  • Hopefully you noticed that $\kappa$ is also a dimensionless variable !
  • There are also many standard dimensionless variables.
  • The one we've had the most experience with is the Reynolds number : \begin{align*} \text{Re}=\frac{\rho \left\langle v\right\rangle D}{\mu} \end{align*}
  • But this quickly led us on to the dimensionless friction factor, which is (for laminar flow) \begin{align*} C_f = 16/\text{Re} \end{align*} which shows that the friction factor is actually just another form of the Reynolds number (for laminar systems).
  • We will later discuss alternative (Metzner-Reed) definitions of the Reynolds number for non-Newtonian fluids ($\text{Re}_{MR}$ ), and also ambiguities in its definition in complex geometries: \begin{align*} \text{Re}_H = \frac{\rho \left\langle v\right\rangle D_H}{\mu}? \end{align*}
  • We have also just reintroduced the Nusselt number : \begin{align*} \text{Nu}=\frac{h L}{k} \end{align*}
  • When we looked for definitions of the Nusselt number in the literature, we then found we needed the Prandtl number, $\text{Pr}$ ! \begin{align*} h&=\frac{k \text{Nu}}{L} & \text{Nu} &\approx C \text{Re}^m \text{Pr}^n \end{align*}
  • Pretty soon we're going to introduce the Grashof number, $\text{Gr}$, and the Raylegih number, $\text{Ra}$ for natural convection.
  • And once we introduce mass diffusion we'll have the Schmidt number, $\text{Sc}$, and the Sherwood number $\text{Sh}$ !
  • But despite the huge range of dimensionless numbers, made worse by ambiguities in complex geometries and non-Newtonian flows etc., we still find them extremely useful, why is this?

The Natural Units?

  • We simply have to ask, what are the units of nature?
  • The simple answer is that units are a totally artificial scale we use to measure reality by and nature has no use for them. That's why there are so many unit systems, they're artificial constructions.
  • Although SI units are the standard unit system, it is just a convenient system and not particularly special or more “natural” in some fundamental way.
  • Physical phenomenon must be independent of the unit systems used to measure them.
  • If natural processes can be described by equations, these equations must somehow eliminate units from playing any role, thus…
  • Dimensionless units are the natural unit system.

Dimensional Analysis

  • Let's take a look at various physical systems and see if dimensional analysis can help us out (the full method is called Buckingham's $\Pi$ -theorem, and has additional method to determine the number of dimensionless variables).
  • Consider the pendulum on the right hand side, if we're designing one, we want to know what its period of oscillation, $t$, is.
  • We already know that the period of a pendulum doesn't depend on the amplitude of the swing.
  • We can guess that its period will be a function of the string length $l$, gravitational acceleration $g$ and the mass $m$. \begin{align*} t = f\left(l,  m,  g\right) \end{align*} where $f$ is an unknown function.
\begin{align*} t = f\left(l,  m,  g\right) \end{align*}
  • We know that physical systems are independent of units, (the period doesn't change even though we measure it in hours or seconds). We should then make the equation independent of the units by making all terms dimensionless: \begin{align*} \frac{t}{T} = f\left(\frac{l}{L},  \frac{m}{M},  \frac{g T^2}{L}\right) \end{align*} where $L$ is the unit of length, $M$ is the unit of mass, and $T$ is the unit of time.
  • $L$, $M$, and $T$ aren't units in the sense of kilograms or meters, but rather parts of the system we decide to make the unit.
  • For example, in a pipe of radius $R,$ we often use the dimensionless variable $r/R,$ where $R$ is the unit length.
\begin{align*} \frac{t}{T} = f\left(\frac{l}{L},  \frac{m}{M},  \frac{g T^2}{L}\right) \end{align*}
  • We need to choose a length, mass, and time scale for the pendulum.
  • The unit length of the system can be the length of the pendulum $L=l$, and the unit mass can be it's mass, $M=m$.
  • We can then get a unit of time from the gravitational constant \begin{align*} T = \sqrt{l/g} \end{align*}
  • Inserting these into the expression above, we have \begin{align*} \frac{t}{\sqrt{l/g}} = f\left(\frac{l}{l},  \frac{m}{m}, \frac{g l}{l g}\right) = f\left(1,  1,  1\right) \end{align*}
  • The unknown function is now a constant!
\begin{align*} \frac{t}{\sqrt{l/g}} =f\left(1,  1,  1\right) \end{align*}
  • We now know that the period of a pendulum doesn't depend on its mass as its dimensionless form is equal to a function of constants (also a constant).
  • We could determine the unknown constant either through experimental observation or through a more careful theoretical analysis.
  • The actual result is \begin{align*} \frac{t}{\sqrt{l/g}} = 2 \pi \end{align*}
  • We can see that dimensional analysis has not only simplified the system, but almost solved it.
  • Even when dimensional analysis can't give us the answer, it can tell us a lot more about the problem.
  • For example, if we want to understand the forced heat transfer coefficient, $h$, we can guess that its a function of the following variables of the flow \begin{align*} h = f\left(d,  \mu,  k,  {\left\langle v \right\rangle},  \rho,  C_p\right) \end{align*} where $d$ is some lengthscale of the system (diameter, channel width).
  • This is a huge, compicated expression, but it can be greatly simplified if we make the system dimensionless: \begin{align*} \frac{h \Theta T^3}{M} = f\left(\frac{d}{L},  \frac{\mu T L}{M},  \frac{k T^3 \Theta}{M L},  \frac{T\left\langle v \right\rangle}{L},  \frac{\rho L^3}{M},  \frac{C_p T^2 \Theta}{L^2}\right) \end{align*} where $\Theta$ is the unit of temperature.
  • Looking at each term, its obvious that it will simplify if we select $L=d$ as the unit length, $M=\rho L^3$ as the unit mass, $T= M/(\mu L)$ as the unit time, and $\Theta=M L/(T^3 k)$ as the unit Temperature.
\begin{align*} \frac{h \Theta T^3}{M} = f\left(\frac{d}{L},  \frac{\mu T L}{M},  \frac{k T^3 \Theta}{M L},  \frac{T\left\langle v \right\rangle}{L},  \frac{\rho L^3}{M},  \frac{C_p T^2 \Theta}{L^2}\right) \end{align*} \begin{align*} L&=d & M&=\rho L^3 & T&= M/(\mu L) & \Theta&=M L/(T^3 k) \end{align*}
  • Inserting in the temperature unit $\Theta$ first, we have: \begin{align*} \frac{h L}{k} = f\left(\frac{d}{L},  \frac{\mu T L}{M},  1,  \frac{T\left\langle v \right\rangle}{L},  \frac{\rho L^3}{M},  \frac{C_p M}{T L k}\right) \end{align*}
  • Inserting the time unit $T$ next, we have \begin{align*} \frac{h L}{k} = f\left(\frac{d}{L},  1,  1,  \frac{M\left\langle v \right\rangle}{\mu L^2},  \frac{\rho L^3}{M},  \frac{C_p \mu}{k}\right) \end{align*}
  • Inserting the mass unit $M$ next, we have \begin{align*} \frac{h L}{k} = f\left(\frac{d}{L},  1,  1,  \frac{\rho\left\langle v \right\rangle L}{\mu},  1,  \frac{C_p \mu}{k}\right) \end{align*}
\begin{align*} \frac{h L}{k} = f\left(\frac{d}{L},  1,  1,  \frac{\rho\left\langle v \right\rangle L}{\mu},  1,  \frac{C_p \mu}{k}\right) \end{align*} \begin{align*} L&=d & M&=\rho L^3 & T&= M/(\mu L) & \Theta&=M L/(T^3 k) \end{align*}
  • Finally, inserting in the length unit $L$, we have: \begin{align*} \frac{h d}{k} = f\left(1,  1,  1,  \frac{\rho\left\langle v \right\rangle d}{\mu},  1,  \frac{C_p \mu}{k}\right) \end{align*}
  • You should notice that the left hand side is the Nusselt number, while the two terms inside the unknown function are the Reynolds number and the Prandtl number!
  • We can then write: \begin{align*} \text{Nu} = \frac{h d}{k} = f\left(\text{Re},  \text{Pr}\right) \end{align*}
\begin{align*} \text{Nu} = \frac{h d}{k} = f\left(\text{Re},  \text{Pr}\right) \end{align*}
  • One of the “known”/engineering expressions for the Nusselt number is \begin{align*} \text{Nu} &\approx C \text{Re}^m \text{Pr}^n \end{align*} This power-law form is used a lot in engineering as you can plot it on log-log paper and the slope gives you the values of $m$ and $n$ while the intercept is $C$. \begin{align*} \ln\text{Nu} &\approx \ln C+ m\ln \text{Re} + n\ln\text{Pr} \end{align*}
  • Although dimensional analysis has not been able to show us the final form of the expression, it has simplified the problem to just two dimensionless variables!
  • This is much simpler than the previous expression! \begin{align*} h = f\left(d,  \mu,  k,  {\left\langle v \right\rangle},  \rho,  C_p\right) \end{align*}
  • Hopefully this can convince you of the importance of dimensionless numbers and the power of dimensional analysis.

The General Approach: Nuclear Tests

  • There is one last example to convince you of its utility.
  • The first nuclear test was carried out in 1945, and was called the “trinity” nuclear test. It had the same conceptual design as the device detonated over Nagasaki.
  • When the photographic data of the trinity test was released in 1950 (after a successful Russian test), G. I. Taylor published a paper where he estimated the bomb's yield.
  • His result (although it has some minor errors) correctly predicted the magnitude as 18 kilotons of TNT.
  • Dimensional analysis allowed him to calculate this result.
  • The declassified photo's were images of the blast, with a time stamp and scale (see right).
  • Let's suppose that the blast radius $r$ is only a function of the blast energy, $e$, the time from the blast, $t$, and the density of air, $\rho$. We then have: \begin{align*} r = f\left(t,  \rho,  e\right) \end{align*}
  • Making the expression dimensionless, we have \begin{align*} \frac{r}{L} = f\left(\frac{t}{T},  \frac{\rho L^3}{M},  \frac{e T^2}{M L^2}\right) \end{align*}
\begin{align*} \frac{r}{L} = f\left(\frac{t}{T},  \frac{\rho L^3}{M},  \frac{e T^2}{M L^2}\right) \end{align*}
  • Lets pick the unit of time to be $T=t$, the unit of mass to be $M=\rho L^3$.
  • Throwing these in, we have \begin{align*} \frac{r}{L} = f\left(1, 1,  \frac{e t^2}{\rho L^5}\right) \end{align*}
  • Now its easy to rearrange for the unit of length $L=\left(\frac{e t^2}{\rho}\right)^{1/5}$, which gives: \begin{align*} r\left(\frac{\rho}{e t^2}\right)^{1/5} = f\left(1, 1, 1\right) = S \end{align*} where the unknown constant is denoted $S$.
\begin{align*} r\left(\frac{\rho}{t^2}\right)^{1/5}e^{-1/5} = S \end{align*}
  • On the day of the test, the density of the air was measured to be $\rho=1$ kg m ${}^3$.
  • We can take two of the photographs released and see what equations come out when we measure the radius and time.
\begin{align*} r\left(\frac{\rho}{t^2}\right)^{1/5} e^{-1/5}= S \end{align*} When the measured blast radius (minus the tower height of 30 m), along with the density and image time, is substituted into the equation, we get the above expressions.
  • These expressions are roughly equivalent, showing the equation is almost correct. The average constant term is given below: \begin{align*} 578 e^{-1/5} = S \end{align*}
  • The energy comes out to be: \begin{align*} e = \left(\frac{578}{S}\right)^5 \end{align*}
  • If we assume the constant term is approximately $S\approx1$ (actual proof is way more complicated here, but we could just use a “known” explosion to calibrate it) we get: \begin{align*} e \approx 578^5 \approx 64\times10^{12} \text{J} = 64 \text{TJ} \end{align*}
  • One ton of TNT releases $4.184\times10^9$ J of energy, therefore our predicted yeild is $15.4$ kilotonnes of TNT!
  • The yeild was in fact, around 18 kilotonnes of TNT.
