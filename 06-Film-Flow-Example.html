Example: Film Flow

  • In the previous lectures, we derived the general balance equations for the mass (known as the continuity equation): \begin{align*} \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} &= -\nabla\cdot \rho \boldsymbol{v} & \text{(Continuity equation)} \end{align*}
  • We also derived the Cauchy momentum equation: \begin{align*} \rho\frac{\partial\boldsymbol{v}}{\partial t} &= -\rho \boldsymbol{v}\cdot \nabla \boldsymbol{v} - \nabla\cdot\boldsymbol{\tau} - \nabla p + \rho \boldsymbol{g} & \text{(Cauchy momentum equation)} \end{align*}
  • We now have all of the mathematical tools needed to study laminar flow problems in rectangular coordinates .
  • Today, we will look at applying these equations to simple flow problems.
  • As an example application of the equations we have derived, we will look at gravity driven flow of a falling film of water down an infinite plate.
  • Although this problem at first sounds abstract, the properties of falling films are crucial in many engineering applicatons.
  • For example, the performance of condensers (covered later in the course) is controlled by the thickness and velocity of the layer of fluid on their surface.
  • If you're a formulation engineer, you may want to ensure paint supports a certain thickness of film before it starts to run. For this, you will need to know the stress as a function of layer thickness.
  • When presented with a new system, there are several steps we should perfom to ensure we have the simplest mathematical description possible.
  • First, we should try to determine the natural coordinates system for the problem and identify any points, axis or planes of symmetry.
  • In this problem, rectangular ($x$, $y$, $z$) as opposed to cylindrical ($r$, $\theta$, $z$) or spherical ($r$, $\theta$, $\phi$) coordinates are a natural choice as the domain or space of the problem is rectangular (and we've only got rectangular equations so far).
  • We should then place the coordinates at a convenient location and orientation in the system.
  • The coordinates should be aligned with any symmetries in the system.
  • Here, the $x$ and $z$ axis are placed in the plane of the bottom boundary.
  • This was done as the problem is symmetric in $z$ (it's a 2D problem) and the flow may be symmetric in $x$…
  • The next task is to identify all of the variables of the system.
  • The variables already identified are the flow velocity $\boldsymbol{v}$ , gravity $\boldsymbol{g}$ , pressure $p$ , and density $\rho$ .
  • We only need to add the thickness of the film $Y$ and inclination of the plane $\theta_{plane}$.
  • The next task is to identify some boundary conditions (BC) of these variables.
  • There are many mathematical categories of boundary conditions, but the two simplest are:
    • Dirichlet or first-type BC: This is a statement of the value of a variable at some boundary of the system. E.g.: \begin{align*} f(x_1) = C_1 && f(x_2) = C_2 \end{align*} This type is useful when dealing with constrained systems where some value is held fixed.
    • Neumann or second-type BC: This is a statement of the first derivative at some boundary. E.g.: \begin{align*} f'(x_1) = C_1 && f'(x_2) = C_2 \end{align*} This is useful when dealing with boundaries with an unknown value but there is some symmetry or other feature of the system which can be exploited.
  • In fluid flow terminology, there are other names for boundary conditions which fit into either or both of these two mathematical classifications.
  • The bottom boundary condition for this system is known as the no-slip boundary condition . The fluid cannot slip on the plate surface and so it's velocity must match that of the plate, which is zero in this system. \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{v}=\boldsymbol{0} & & \text{for $y=0$} \end{align*}
  • This is a Dirichlet or first-type boundary condition as it constrains the value of the velocity at the boundary.
  • There is another boundary condition type, known as the slip/symmetry condition. This states that there is no stress at the boundary. \begin{align*} \tau_{ij} &= 0 \end{align*}
  • Again, this is a Dirichlet or first-type boundary condition, but in the stress.
  • In many fluids, the stress is proportional to the velocity gradient, so this is a Neumann or second-type boundary condition in the velocity.
  • Please keep in mind for the future that you also get the same effect from a symmetry condition as typically there is no transport across a plane of symmetry, as there is no gradient/driving-force across the point, axis, or plane of symmetry.
  • Back to the problem in hand: The top boundary condition is difficult to specify as the fluid velocity is not constrained by a boundary or wall.
  • If we were being completely precise, we would have to equate the stresses of the Air and Water phases at this point (each force has an equal and opposite reactionary force). \begin{align*} \tau_{xy,water}(Y) = \tau_{xy,air}(Y) \end{align*}
  • But the viscosity of air at STP is $\mu_{air}=0.018$ centipoise and that of water is $\mu_{water}=1$ centipoise. The ratio is $\mu_{water}/\mu_{air}=56$ .
  • This means that only a small amount of stress is exerted by the air as it is not very viscous and we can assume that the actual stress is negligible: \begin{align*} \tau_{xy,water}(Y) = 0 \end{align*}
  • As we discussed previously, this boundary condition is a Dirichlet or first-type boundary condition in the stress as we specify the value of the stress at the boundary. \begin{align*} \tau_{xy,water}(Y) = 0 \end{align*}
  • However, we're interested in the velocity $\boldsymbol{v}$ and (as water is a Newtonian fluid) they are related by \begin{align*} \tau_{xy,water} &= -\mu \left(\frac{\partial v_x}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial v_y}{\partial x}\right) \end{align*} Now we can assume $v_y=0$ if the flow is laminar (not turblent) and we're far from any entrance/exits/side-walls and their effects, then we have $\tau_{xy,water}=0$ at the interface implies ${\partial v_y}/{\partial x}=0$.
  • It is now obvious that a Dirichlet boundary condition in the stress is a Neumann or second-type boundary condition in the velocity!
  • This is the summary of our problem so far, we have an appropriate coordinate system, all of the relevant variables defined, and sufficient boundary conditions.
  • We now need to examine the balance equations, starting with the continuity equation. \begin{align*} \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot \rho \boldsymbol{v} = 0 \end{align*}
\begin{align*} \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \nabla \cdot \rho \boldsymbol{v} = 0 \end{align*}
  • We will assume the flow is steady state and far from any disturbance (so there's no unsteady or swirling flow from the entry/exit effects or side walls): \begin{align*} \cancelto{0}{\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t}} + \nabla \cdot \rho \boldsymbol{v} = 0 \end{align*}
  • Water is quite incompressible, so we can assume the density is constant. Dividing by the density yields: \begin{align*} \nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{v} &= 0 \end{align*}
  • We will also assume the flow is laminar so there is no flow in the y-direction due to turbulence. There is also no flow in the z-direction as the flow is 2D (symmetric in the z component).
  • Expanding the equation and cancelling the velocities in the y and z directions as we're laminar, steady-state, and far from disturbances: \begin{align*} \nabla \cdot \boldsymbol{v} = \nabla_y \cancelto{0}{v_y} + \nabla_z \cancelto{0}{v_z} + \nabla_x v_x &= 0 \end{align*}
\begin{align*} \nabla_x v_x = \frac{\partial v_x}{\partial r_x} = \frac{\partial v_x}{\partial x} = 0 \end{align*}
  • This statement simply means that the velocity profile will not vary in the x direction (along the plate)!
  • This is an important result as it means that we only need to solve the problem at one location and the solution is valid for the length of the plate.
  • We will also use this result to simplify the Cauchy momentum equation…
  • Our problem is beginning to look “well-posed” (all of the boundary conditions, equations and variables are defined). But we need one more equation to complete it.
  • Let's look at the Cauchy momentum balance for the system: \begin{align*} \rho\frac{\partial\boldsymbol{v}}{\partial t} &= -\rho \boldsymbol{v}\cdot \nabla\boldsymbol{v} - \nabla\cdot\boldsymbol{\tau} - \nabla p + \rho \boldsymbol{g} \end{align*}
  • In index notation, the Cauchy equation is written as: \begin{align*} \rho\frac{\partial v_i}{\partial t} &= -\rho v_j\nabla_j v_i - \nabla_j\tau_{ji} - \nabla_i p + \rho g_i \end{align*}

    Math Notes

    The dot/scalar product of a matrix and a vector is defined as: \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{a}\cdot\boldsymbol{B} &= a_i b_{ij} & \boldsymbol{B}\cdot\boldsymbol{a} = b_{ji}a_i \end{align*}
  • We can immediately cancel the time derivative as the system is at steady state: \begin{align*} \rho\cancelto{0}{\frac{\partial v_i}{\partial t}} &= -\rho v_j\nabla_j v_i - \nabla_j\tau_{ji} - \nabla_i p + \rho g_i \end{align*}

ATTENTION! PLACE OF STUDENT DIFFICULTY!

\begin{align*} \rho v_j\nabla_j v_i = -\nabla_j\tau_{ji} - \nabla_i p + \rho g_i \end{align*}
  • We need to do a lot of simplification here and all of it centres around the interpretation of $\boldsymbol{\tau}$ and $\nabla$ .
  • First, whenever a variable (e.g., velocity) doesn't change as you travel in a certain direction, the derivative in that direction is zero.
  • In this problem, thanks to our assumptions, nothing changes in the $y$ or $z$-directions, therefore any term with a $\nabla_z=\partial/\partial z$ or $\nabla_x=\partial/\partial x$ term is zero! Very powerful!
  • Any repeated indices loop over all dimensions, but above we can immediately replace $j$ with $y$, as any other value is zero!

ATTENTION! PLACE OF STUDENT DIFFICULTY!

\begin{align*} \rho \cancelto{0}{v_y}\nabla_y v_i = -\nabla_y\tau_{yi} - \nabla_i p + \rho g_i \end{align*}
  • But, this means the first term is completely zero as this also forces the leading $v$ term to become $v_y=0$!
  • The stress $\tau_{ji}$ is given by \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{\tau}=\tau_{ji} = -\mu \left(\frac{\partial v_j}{\partial r_i} + \frac{\partial v_i}{\partial r_j}\right) + \delta_{ij} \mu^B \cancelto{0}{\nabla\cdot\boldsymbol{v}} \end{align*} where $\nabla\cdot\boldsymbol{v}=0$ from the continuity equation earlier.
  • We're only interested in $\tau_{yi}$ (so $j=y$), but only $v_x$ is non-zero, so only for $i=x$ is the term $\nabla_y\,\tau_{yi}$ non-zero, in every other case it is zero! You will need to go through this yourself to see it.
\begin{align*} -\nabla_y\tau_{yx} &= \nabla_x  p - \rho g_x & 0 &= \nabla_y  p - \rho g_y & 0 &= \nabla_z  p - \rho \cancelto{0}{g_z} \end{align*}
  • So we're down to three equations (see above). Gravity doesn't act in the $z$-direction so we can cancel it in the $z$ equation. This $z$ equation tells you that pressure doesn't vary in the $z$-direction, i.e., $\nabla_z\,p=0$, which is not surprising.
  • The $y$-equation simply says that pressure varies according to a constant. If you integrate this you will find the hydrostatic pressure law, \begin{align*} \nabla_y p=\rho g_y \rightarrow p(y)=\rho g_y y+p_0. \end{align*} where $p_0$ is the pressure at $y=0$ and $g_y=-\left|\boldsymbol{g}\right|\sin\theta_{plane}$
  • Both of these things we know already, so in the future we'll just go straight to the flow direction, $x$.
  • We're going to solve for the stress $\tau_{xy}$. We will ignore $\nabla_x\,p$, as its just the hydrostatic pressure of air change along the slope, which is a small change over the height of our film flows. We can integrate the expression for the stress to yield: \begin{align*} \tau_{yx} = \rho g_x y + C_1 \end{align*}
  • The integration constant is found using the boundary condition $\tau_{xy}(Y)=0$ , therefore $C_1 =-\rho g_x Y$ and: \begin{align*} \tau_{yx} = \rho g_x\left(y-Y\right) \end{align*}
  • Although the stress is useful, we require information on the flow velocity.
  • The stress is linked to the velocity through a viscous model. Water is a Newtonian fluid, and we have the following 3D definition of Newton's law of viscosity: \begin{align*} \tau_{ij} = -\mu \left(\frac{\partial v_i}{\partial r_j} + \frac{\partial v_j}{\partial r_i}\right) - \delta_{ij} \mu^B \nabla\cdot\boldsymbol{v} \end{align*}
  • We're interested in $\tau_{yx}$ , and as $v_x$ is the only non-zero velocity, we have: \begin{align*} \tau_{yx} = -\mu \left(\cancelto{0}{\frac{\partial v_y}{\partial x}} + \frac{\partial v_x}{\partial y}\right) - \cancelto{0}{\delta_{yx}} \mu^B \cancelto{0}{\nabla\cdot\boldsymbol{v}} = -\mu\frac{\partial v_x}{\partial y} \end{align*}
\begin{align*} \tau_{yx} &= \rho g_x\left(y - Y\right) = -\mu\frac{\partial v_x}{\partial y} \end{align*}
  • We can combine these two expressions for the stress and integrate it to obtain an expression for the flow velocity: \begin{align*} \frac{\partial v_x}{\partial y} &= \mu^{-1}\rho  g_x\left(Y - y\right)\\ v_x(y) &= \mu^{-1} \rho g_x\left(Y y - \frac{y^2}{2}\right) + C_2 \end{align*}
  • From the no-slip condition we have $v_x(0) = 0$ , which means $C_2=0$ and yields the final expression: \begin{align*} v_x(y) &= \mu^{-1}\rho  g_x\left(Y y - \frac{y^2}{2}\right) \end{align*}
  • We have found the pressure, stress and flow velocity profile of a film falling down a plate.
  • However there are several other properties of the flow which are interesting to us as engineers.

Volumetric Flow Rate

  • To find the volumetric flow rate of a fluid we just have to integrate the flow velocity across the flow channel. \begin{align*} \dot{V}_x = \iint v_x  {\rm d}y  {\rm d}z \end{align*}
  • In the trivial case of a constant flow rate this becomes the familiar expression: \begin{align*} \dot{V}_x = v_x  A_{yz} \end{align*} where $A_{yz}$ is the cross sectional area of the flow in the $yz$ -plane.
  • In the case of our falling film we have: \begin{align*} \dot{V}_x &= \int_0^Z\int_0^Y v_x  {\rm d}y  {\rm d}z\\ &= \int_0^Z\int_0^Y\mu^{-1}\rho g_x\left(Y y - \frac{y^2}{2}\right) {\rm d}y  {\rm d}z\\ &= \left[\mu^{-1}\rho g_x\left(Y \frac{y^2}{2} - \frac{y^3}{6}\right)Z\right]_{0}^{Y} \end{align*}

Volumetric Flow Rate

  • Carrying on… \begin{align*} \dot{V}_x &= \left[\mu^{-1}\rho g_x\left(Y \frac{y^2}{2} - \frac{y^3}{6}\right)Z\right]_{0}^{Y}\\ &=\mu^{-1}\rho g_x Y^3 Z\left(\frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{6}\right)\\ \dot{V}_x&=\frac{\rho g_x Y^3 Z}{3 \mu} \end{align*}
  • The volumetric flow rate is a key design variable when looking at flow in pipelines, heat exchangers/condensers/boilers or other hydrodynamic processes.

Average Velocity

  • Another useful property is the average flow velocity, given by: \begin{align*} \left\langle v_x\right\rangle = \frac{\dot{V}_x}{A_{yz}} \end{align*}
  • In our falling film example it is given by: \begin{align*} \left\langle v_x\right\rangle = \frac{\dot{V}_x}{Y Z} = \frac{\rho g_x Y^2}{3 \mu} \end{align*}
