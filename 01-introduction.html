Introduction

  • Engineering is the translation of the real world into mathematics.
  • This has to be done to gain certainty about design, anything else is guesswork.
  • We do this informally all the time:
    • Q: How long will it take to drive to Edinburgh which is 128 miles away?
    • A: Assuming we drive at $60~\text{MPH}$, it will take $S/V=128/60\approx2~\text{h}$.
  • The essence of good engineering is choosing the right approximations, making sure you overdesign and not underdesign (i.e., don't choose $70~\text{MPH}$).
  • Design is never-ending, you can always improve it (i.e., account for traffic, weather, etc.)
  • You will need experience to help you decide what are acceptable approximations.
  • Many models/approximations are differential, not algebraic like above, this course gives you experience in solving these types of systems.
  • Q: A skydiver has lept from a plane, what is their terminal velocity and how do they approach it?
  • Assume from experience that drag forces are of the form $F^{drag}=k\,v^2$ where $k$ is a drag coefficient.
  • We already know from experience that Newton's law applies, \begin{align*} F = m\,a = m\frac{{\rm d} v}{{\rm d} t} = F^{gravity}+F^{drag}\\ \frac{1}{g+\frac{k}{m}\,v^2}\frac{{\rm d} v}{{\rm d} t} = 1\\ v = \sqrt{g\,m\,k^{-1}}\tanh\left(t\sqrt{g\,k\,m^{-1}}\right)=\sqrt{g\,m\,k^{-1}}\frac{1-e^{-2\,t\sqrt{g\,k\,m^{-1}}}}{1+e^{-2\,t\sqrt{g\,k\,m^{-1}}}} \end{align*}
  • Differential equations are required here (and in most engineering problems) and they will test your math knowledge!
  • In this course, the problems are selected to test only a limited (but non-trivial) range of mathematics. You will need to practice the covered maths, and future courses will expand on this!
  • A large part of this course will be setting up the differential equations (i.e., where did Newton's law come from?).
  • There are too many systems out there to cover this in general, so why Heat, Mass, and Momentum in particular?
    • They have very similar properties, they all flow and conduct/diffuse.
    • This is because they are the conserved quantities of a system.
      They do not rapidly change or fluctuate but move from regions of high concentration to low concentration.
  • Almost every engineering system centers around the transport of momentum, heat, and mass.
  • These systems may utilise one or all of these modes of transport to achieve a desired effect.
  • For example, nuclear reactors need effective heat transport to cool and extract energy from the fission reaction.
  • But understanding the heat transfer will also require understanding the mass transfer of coolant, resulting from a transport of momentum.
  • Mass can also transfer via diffusion through the walls, but we'd be designing to minimise this particular transport.
  • To perform our jobs as Engineers, we need to be able generate equations that describe all of the modes of any relevant system.
  • We need to be able to generate analytical or semi-empirical expressions (design equations) that tell use how the system will behave.
  • For example, we need to know how high the driving pressure drop must be to cool the nuclear reactor as a function of reactor dimensions.
  • We need to know how the thickness of the containment walls will affect (slow) the heat transfer.
  • How can we obtain these design equations?
  • You should already be familiar of some of the simple ways in which we can treat transport phenomena from your earlier courses.
  • For conduction of heat, we use the integral form of Fourier's law. \begin{align*} Q &= U\,A\,\Delta T\end{align*}
  • The problem with this expression is its only valid for very simple systems (like a flat wall).
  • However, as its name suggests, it is the integrated form of differential form of Fourier's law. \begin{align*} q &= -k\frac{dT}{dx}\end{align*}
  • We should be able to take the more general differential form and apply it to any heat transfer problem...
  • We also need to approach heat transfer problems through Energy balances. \begin{align*} (Q_{in} - Q_{out})\Delta t &= m\,C_P\Delta T\end{align*}
  • Again, this is just an integrated form of a differential energy balance: \begin{align*} q_{in} - q_{out} &= \rho\,C_P \frac{dT}{dt}\end{align*}
    • Transport phenomena is the study of using {\em differential} constitutive relationships (e.g., Fourier's law)) and {\em differential} balances to generate engineering expressions for any system.
  • It's important to note that transport phenomena is more general than heat transfer.
  • You should be familiar with the transport equations of basic fluid flow.
  • For example, Bernoulli's equation is an energy balance of a flow: \begin{align*}\frac{1}{2}\rho\,v^2 + p - \rho\,g\,h\end{align*} \begin{align*}E_{kinetic} + E_{pressure} + E_\text{grav. potential}\end{align*}
  • And there is the Hagen-Poiseuille equation for flow in a pipe, relating the volumetric flow-rate, $\dot{V}$, to the pressure drop, $\Delta p$. \begin{align*} \dot{V}_z= -\pi\frac{\Delta p}{L}\frac{R^4}{8\,\mu} \end{align*}
  • Later in the course, we will show that Bernoulli's equation can be derived from a more general momentum balance equation: \begin{align*} \frac{\partial \boldsymbol{v}}{\partial t} &= -\boldsymbol{v}\cdot\nabla \boldsymbol{v} + \rho^{-1}\nabla\cdot\boldsymbol{\tau} - \rho^{-1}\nabla p + \boldsymbol{g} \end{align*}
  • If we then use a constitutive relation for momentum, such as Newton's law of viscosity: \begin{align*} \tau_{xy} = -\mu \frac{\partial v_x}{\partial y} \end{align*} we can then derive the Hagen-Poiseuille expression for flow in pipes.
  • Again, we can combine a constitutive relationship (Newton's law of viscosity) with a differential balance (momentum balance) to solve flow problems using the study of Transport Phenomena.

Transport Phenomena are usually limited to simple geometries and problems:

  • What to do when the treatment becomes complex? For example, boiling liquids are complex heat transfer and multiphase flow problems:
    • empirical expressions to be able to perform engineering calculations on the system.
  • However, the best empirical expressions are usually based somehow on the simpler analytical results.
  • In this course we will look at both analytical results from Transport Phenomena, and empirical “fudges” for more complex systems.
