Energy Balances

  • In previous lectures we derived and applied the mass balance equation, AKA the continuity equation. \begin{align*} \frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} &=- \nabla\cdot \rho \boldsymbol{v} \end{align*}
  • The continuity equation currently leads to some relatively trivial results ($IN=OUT$ ) for one-dimensional fluid systems, so we have derived the momentum balance equation: \begin{align*} \rho\frac{\partial\boldsymbol{v}}{\partial t} &= -\rho \boldsymbol{v}\cdot \nabla\boldsymbol{v} - \nabla\cdot\boldsymbol{\tau} - \nabla p + \rho \boldsymbol{g} \end{align*}
  • Using these two equations, we have studied a range of simplified and complex problems in fluid flow of incompressible Newtonian fluids. We will later extend to multi-phase and non-Newtonian fluids.
  • We have shown how to attack many fluid flow/momentum transport problem from a solid basis, accounting for all assumptions along the way.
  • Can we extend what we've studied to other “transport” problems?
  • We have found that the study of momentum and mass transport allows us to derive design equations for hydraulic systems.
  • But hydraulic design is only one half of many Chemical/Petroleum processes…
  • …thermal design is an essential part of all processes (see the insulation on the pipeline to the right).
  • Often, heat transfer is integral to the treatment of process fluids.
  • For example, the pasteurisation process for milk requires the milk to be rapidly heated to $71.7^\circ$C to kill any bacteria, then rapidly cooled to prevent the milk burning.
  • Even in 2011, “Raw milk is responsible for nearly three times more hospitalizations than any other foodborne disease outbreak, making it one of the world's most dangerous food products.” 1
  • Flow and heat transfer must be carefully controlled and designed to facilitate this process, the stakes are high!
1http://edition.cnn.com/2011/10/12/health/food-poisoning-protection-guide/index.html
  • Heat transfer also drives all separation processes and many reactions (must transfer heat to "pay" any entropy cost). \begin{align*} {\rm d}S_{\rm sys} \geq \frac{{\partial}Q}{T_{\rm surr}} \end{align*}
  • Distillation columns are a great example, which I teach ChemE next term (see EX3502 Separation processes 1).
  • The input of heat into the heat exchanger at the base (the re-boiler) partially boils the fluid and produces a vapour.
  • The removal of heat in the heat exchanger at the top (the condenser) produces a liquid stream.
  • The contacting counter-current flow of these liquid and vapour streams through the column cause it to separate a mixture into it's component fluids.
  • What do heat exchangers look like?
  • A very common flow configuration of a heat exchanger is the shell-and-tube exchanger.
  • In such systems, one process fluid passes through the shell-side of an exchanger, flowing around a tube-bundle.
  • In the tube-side another fluid passes, and heat is exchanged through the walls of the tubes.
  • Plate heat-exchangers are the other most popular design and are quite different.
  • To design a heat exchanger, we must understand the flow of the fluids on either side of the heat exchanger (flow in pipes and between plates), to size any pumps/compressors required to "pay" the pressure drop.
  • The key function of a heat-exchanger is the transport of energy which takes place at the same time as the transport of momentum.
  • We will will need a differential balance equation which describes the transport of energy, so we can design this aspect of heat exchange, but this will include contributions from the kinetic energy of the flow (this is why we cover momentum first!).
  • When we talk about the energy $e$ (energy per unit mass) of a substance, there are several ways in which that the energy may be stored in a substance: \begin{align*} e = … \end{align*}
  • We need to find expressions for the concentration of all of the types of energy that are stored in a given mass of fluid. We need the concentration $c_\text{Energy}$ for our general balance equation: \begin{align*} \frac{\partial c_B}{\partial t} = -\nabla\cdot \boldsymbol{J}_B + \sigma_B \end{align*}
\begin{align*} e = … \end{align*}
  • The kinetic energy of a fluid is perhaps the most obvious form.
  • The kinetic energy per unit mass is given by \begin{align*} \frac{1}{2}\boldsymbol{v}\cdot\boldsymbol{v} = \frac{1}{2}v^2 \end{align*}
  • This is just the vector and per-unit-mass form of the standard expression, $m v^2/2$ , for the kinetic energy.
\begin{align*} e = \frac{1}{2}v^2 + … \end{align*}
  • Another type of energy are the potential energies which arise from external fields, i.e., $e_{pot.}(\boldsymbol{r})$ .
  • One typical example is the gravitational potential \begin{align*} e^{g}_{pot.}(\boldsymbol{r}) = - \boldsymbol{g}\cdot\boldsymbol{r} \end{align*}
  • This is just the vector and per-unit-mass expression for the standard expression, $\rho g H$.
  • Other examples of potential energies are magnetic/electrical fields, but we do not consider magnetohydrodynamics in this course.
\begin{align*} e = \frac{1}{2}v^2 + e_{pot.}(\boldsymbol{r}) + … \end{align*}
  • To include the chemical and thermal energy of a fluid, the specific thermodynamic internal energy, $U$, must also be included (also in units of energy per unit mass).
  • The most important aspect of this energy for non-reactive flows is from the temperature of the fluid.
  • The heat from reactions is not included in this course, as the hydrodynamics becomes significantly more complex, but it is a challenging aspect of reactor design (see thermal runaway).
  • The total amount of energy per unit mass is given by the sum of these terms \begin{align*} e = \frac{1}{2}v^2 + e_{pot.}(\boldsymbol{r}) + U \end{align*}
  • The concentration of energy (energy per unit volume) is given by \begin{align*} c_{energy} = \rho e \end{align*}
  • Let's take a look at our general balance equation.
\begin{align*} \frac{\partial c_{energy}}{\partial t} = -\nabla\cdot \boldsymbol{J}_{energy} + \sigma_{energy} \end{align*}
  • We've taken the general balance for a quantity, $B$ , and relabeled it for the energy.
  • We already have a definition of the concentration of energy, $c_{energy} = \rho e$ …
  • … If we want to completely define a general balance equation for the energy, we need to find expressions for the energy flux $\boldsymbol{J}_{energy}$ and to include any general sources of energy $\sigma_{energy}$.
  • The first law of thermodynamics states that energy is not created or destroyed, so at first it looks like $\sigma_{energy} = 0$.
  • But it is not always that simple, we might have a nuclear reaction producing heat or heat energy inputted electrically.
  • We will assume that we might have $\sigma_{energy}\neq0$, until we make any further approximations.
\begin{align*} \frac{\partial \rho e}{\partial t} = -\nabla\cdot \boldsymbol{J}_{energy} + \sigma_{energy} \end{align*}
  • Starting with the fluxes of energy in the system, $\boldsymbol{J}_{energy}$ .
  • Here we can identify two fluxes which have direct analogues to the momentum fluxes, the convective and diffusive fluxes of energy.
\begin{align*} \boldsymbol{J}_{energy} = … \end{align*}
  • The convective flux has the same form as both other balances: \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{J}_{mass}^{conv.} &= \left(\rho\right)\boldsymbol{v} \\ \boldsymbol{J}_{mom.}^{conv.} &= \left(\rho  \boldsymbol{v}\right)\boldsymbol{v} \\ \boldsymbol{J}_{energy}^{conv.} &= (\rho e)\boldsymbol{v} \end{align*}
  • We can then immediately write down the convective flux of energy
  • This is the transport of energy due to the movement of the fluid (energy per unit area per second).
\begin{align*} \boldsymbol{J}_{energy} = \boldsymbol{J}_{energy}^{conv.} + … \end{align*}
  • In the derivation of the momentum balance equation, we also had diffusive flux of momentum \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{J}_{mom.}^{diff.} = \boldsymbol{\tau} \end{align*}
  • This is the transport of momentum due to viscous forces in the fluid.
  • But what is the analogy of diffusive energy transport?
\begin{align*} \boldsymbol{J}_{energy} = \boldsymbol{J}_{energy}^{conv.} + … \end{align*}
  • The answer is thermal conduction ! \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{J}_{energy}^{diff.} = \boldsymbol{q} \end{align*}
  • $\boldsymbol{q}$ is known as the heat flux. Assuming the material is isotropic (same in all directions), we have the following “law” \begin{align*} q_x = -k \frac{\partial T}{\partial x} \end{align*} where $k$ is the thermal conductivity.
  • This is Fourier's law.
\begin{align*} \boldsymbol{J}_{energy} = \boldsymbol{J}_{energy}^{conv.} + \boldsymbol{J}_{energy}^{diff.} \end{align*}
  • We should take the time to note the similarity between our momentum and energy balance equations so far.
  • Both the momentum and energy balance have convective fluxes. \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{J}_{mom.}^{conv.} &= \left(\rho \boldsymbol{v}\right)\boldsymbol{v} & \boldsymbol{J}_{energy}^{conv.} &= (\rho e)\boldsymbol{v} \end{align*}
  • And both have diffusive fluxes \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{J}_{mom.}^{diff.} &= \boldsymbol{\tau} & \boldsymbol{J}_{energy}^{diff.} &= \boldsymbol{q} \end{align*}
  • They also both have constitutive relationships to describe the diffusive flux, which both have a driving force and a phenomenological coefficient. \begin{align*} \tau_{ij} &= -\color{red}\mu\color{black} \left(\color{blue}\frac{\partial v_i}{\partial r_j}\color{black}+\color{blue}\frac{\partial v_j}{\partial r_i}\color{black}\right) & q_i &= -\color{red}k\color{black} \color{blue}\frac{\partial T}{\partial r_i}\color{black} \end{align*}
\begin{align*} \boldsymbol{J}_{energy} = \boldsymbol{J}_{energy}^{conv.} + \boldsymbol{J}_{energy}^{diff.} \end{align*}
  • The major difference is that energy is a scalar quantity and momentum is a vector.
  • This means that the transport of energy is simpler than the Cauchy momentum equation, as we only have one equation to solve.
  • But we should expect similar solutions from the two equations.
  • In fact, in certain cases we expect exactly the same answers. It turns out the solution of electrical heating of a wire is equivalent to flow in a pipe!
  • This is what makes the study of Transport Phenomena so powerful, we can set and understand all transport on a common basis.
  • There is a final set of terms that we have neglected in our derivation so far.
  • These terms account for the work performed by the fluid.
  • Work was originally defined in 1824 by Sadi Carnot:

    Reflections on the Motive Power of Fire

    We use here motive power (work) to express the useful effect that a motor is capable of producing… It has, as we know, as a measure, the product of the weight multiplied by the height to which it is raised.
  • Little has changed, aside from a clarification of weight versus force.
  • The rate of work generation is then a force multiplied by a velocity.
  • Work is not really a flux or a source term, more a transformation term, so we'll just add it on as another form of transport. Every force must have a work term!
  • We must examine the forces acting on our control volume and determine how fast the fluid is moving against them to determine the work performed.
  • A simple example of a work balance is given by the pressure. \begin{align*} \text{Work Rate}^{pressure}_{x} + \text{Work Rate}^{pressure}_{x+\Delta x} = \left(p(x) v(x) - p(x+\Delta x) v(x+\Delta x)\right)\Delta y \Delta z \end{align*} (Note, compression adds energy but expansion removes it from the system, and the sign change arises from positive $v(x)$ being a compression, whereas positive $v(x+\Delta x)$ is a expansion).
  • Dividing by the volume $\Delta x \Delta y \Delta z$ and taking the limit $\Delta x \to 0$, $\Delta y \to 0$, $\Delta z \to 0$, we find that the rate of pressure work per unit volume per time is given by \begin{align*} W_{pressure} = -\frac{\partial p v_i}{\partial r_i} = -\nabla_i p v_i = -\nabla\cdot\left(p \boldsymbol{v}\right) \end{align*}
  • There is one other set of forces in a fluid, and these are viscous forces.
  • For each element of the stress tensor $\boldsymbol{\tau}$ (each element represents a stress force) we have a rate of work being performed. \begin{multline*} \text{Work Rate}^{viscous}_{x,xy} + \text{Work Rate}^{viscous}_{x+\Delta x,xy} \\= \left(\tau_{xy}(x) v_y(x)- \tau_{xy}(x+\Delta x) v_y(x+\Delta x) \right) \Delta y  \Delta z \end{multline*}
  • Again, dividing by the volume $\Delta x \Delta y \Delta z$ and taking the limit $\Delta x \to 0$, $\Delta y \to 0$, $\Delta z \to 0$, we find that the rate of viscous work per unit volume per time is given by \begin{align*} W_{viscous}=-\frac{\partial \tau_{ij}v_j}{\partial r_i} = -\nabla_i\tau_{ij}v_j = -\nabla\cdot\boldsymbol{\tau}\cdot\boldsymbol{v} \end{align*}
  • Putting this all together we have \begin{align*} \frac{\partial \rho  e}{\partial t} &= - \nabla_i(\rho e v_i) - \nabla_i q_i - \nabla_i(\tau_{ij} v_j) - \nabla_i(p v_i) + \sigma_{energy} \end{align*}
  • When we derived the momentum balance equation, we managed to factor out the continuity equation.
  • We can do this again here, using the product rule \begin{align*} e\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} + \rho\frac{\partial e}{\partial t} &= - e \nabla_i(\rho v_i) - \rho v_i \nabla_i e - \nabla_i q_i - \nabla_i(\tau_{ij} v_j) - \nabla_i(p v_i) + \sigma_{energy} \\ \rho\frac{\partial e}{\partial t} &= - e\cancelto{0}{\left(\frac{\partial \rho}{\partial t} +\nabla_i(\rho v_i)\right)} - \rho v_i \nabla_i e - \nabla_i q_i - \nabla_i(\tau_{ij} v_j) - \nabla_i(p v_i) + \sigma_{energy} \\ \rho\frac{\partial e}{\partial t} &= - \rho v_i \nabla_i e - \nabla_i q_i - \nabla_i(\tau_{ij} v_j) - \nabla_i(p v_i) + \sigma_{energy} \end{align*}
  • We now have the fundamental balance equation for heat transfer!
  • Apart from the work terms, the derivation is identical to the Cauchy momentum equation.
\begin{align*} \rho\frac{\partial e}{\partial t} &= - \rho v_i \nabla_i e - \nabla_i q_i - \nabla_i(\tau_{ij} v_j) - \nabla_i(p v_i) + \sigma_{energy} \end{align*}
  • Although this is the general equation for thermal transport, this equation greatly simplifies for many interesting cases.
  • For example, lets consider heat conduction in a motionless-fluid or solid ($\boldsymbol{v}=0$ ).
  • Ignoring gravity and any heat source, we have $e=U$ , and the energy balance equation reduces to \begin{align*} \rho \frac{\partial U}{\partial t} = -\nabla\cdot\boldsymbol{q} \end{align*}
  • Assuming the internal energy is only weakly dependent on the pressure, and a constant heat capacity, we can use the thermodynamic relation ${\rm d}U = C_v {\rm d}T$ and Fourier's law with constant thermal conductivity to write \begin{align*} \rho C_v \frac{\partial T}{\partial t} = -\nabla\cdot\boldsymbol{q} = k \nabla^2 T \end{align*}
\begin{align*} \rho C_v \frac{\partial T}{\partial t} = -\nabla\cdot\boldsymbol{q} = k \nabla^2 T \end{align*}
  • This is an important equation called the heat equation and it lets you study many unsteady-state heat transfer problems.
  • In the case of steady state heat conduction, the above equation reduces to \begin{align*} \nabla^2 T = 0 \end{align*}
  • This enormously simple equation is the Laplace equation for steady state heat conduction, which you will encounter repeatedly.
  • In rectangular coordinates, the gradient operator $\nabla$ and the Laplace operator $\nabla^2$ are defined like so. \begin{align*} \nabla s &= \left[\frac{\partial s}{\partial x},  \frac{\partial s}{\partial y},  \frac{\partial s}{\partial z}\right] \\ \nabla^2 s = \nabla_i \nabla_i  s &= \frac{\partial^2 s}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2 s}{\partial y^2} + \frac{\partial^2 s}{\partial z^2} \end{align*}
  • But in cylindrical coordinate systems we have \begin{align*} \nabla s &= \left[\frac{\partial s}{\partial r},  \frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial s}{\partial \theta},  \frac{\partial s}{\partial z}\right] \\ \nabla^2 s &= \frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial}{\partial r}\left(r\frac{\partial s}{\partial r}\right) + \frac{1}{r^2}\frac{\partial^2 s}{\partial \theta^2} + \frac{\partial^2 s}{\partial z^2} \end{align*}
  • Note that you cannot define a $\nabla$ in cylindrical coordinates like so \begin{align*} \nabla &= \left[\nabla_r, \nabla_\theta,  \nabla_z\right] \neq \left[\frac{\partial}{\partial r},  \frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial}{\partial \theta},  \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\right] \end{align*} as when it acts on vector terms (including other $\nabla$ operators) the result is not straightforward…
  • This is because the directions $\hat{\boldsymbol{r}}$ and $\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ , depend on the angle $\theta$.
  • See Appendix A.7 of “Transport Phenomena,” for full details, but the idea is that each vector can be written as the sum of its components along its “basis” vectors: \begin{align*} \boldsymbol{v} =\hat{\boldsymbol{e}}_r \boldsymbol{v}_r + \hat{\boldsymbol{e}}_\theta \boldsymbol{v}_\theta + \hat{\boldsymbol{e}}_z \boldsymbol{v}_z \end{align*}
  • If we then look at the application of $\nabla$ on a vector, we have: \begin{align*} \nabla\cdot\boldsymbol{v} = \left(\hat{\boldsymbol{e}}_r \nabla_r + \hat{\boldsymbol{e}}_\theta \nabla_\theta + \hat{\boldsymbol{e}}_z \nabla_z\right) \cdot \left(\hat{\boldsymbol{e}}_r \boldsymbol{v}_r + \hat{\boldsymbol{e}}_\theta \boldsymbol{v}_\theta + \hat{\boldsymbol{e}}_z \boldsymbol{v}_z\right) \end{align*}
  • The unexpected terms arise as the cross terms, such as $\nabla_\theta\cdot\hat{\boldsymbol{e}}_r$ are not zero, as the direction $\hat{\boldsymbol{e}}_r$ is a function of $\theta$.
  • Rather than follow through these derivatives carefully, we will use look-up tables.
\begin{align*} \boldsymbol{q} &= -k \nabla T & \nabla &=\left[\frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial}{\partial r} r,\quad\frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial}{\partial \theta},\quad \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\right] \end{align*}
